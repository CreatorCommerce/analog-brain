Marketplace does not mean a generic event directory.

Analog's marketplace helps a Sponsor decide three things:

- where its dollars should go
- how visible the campaign should be
- what proof and review context will show whether the spend worked for that Sponsor category

Read this after [Platform Logic](/references/platform-logic) and [AI Memory And Context](/platform/ai-memory-and-context).

Platform Logic explains the current surfaces. AI Memory And Context explains how product usage, recursive docs, and public market signals become bounded agent context.

This page explains the marketplace intelligence those surfaces should build toward.

In active `cc-analog`, the public directory is the first marketplace input, not the whole marketplace. It makes public-safe supply legible through listings, categories, lists, search, reviews, bookmarks, claim state, volunteer signals, grant readiness, and Host-owned listing updates. The Sponsor-side matching, campaign visibility, collab, spend, benchmark, and review layers below are preserved full-platform logic until restored from archived code/docs.

```text
Sponsor goal + category + budget
-> campaign visibility mode
-> AI-assisted match to receptive Hosts, communities, Venues, and event types
-> governed spend and vendor record handling
-> benchmarked proof
-> two-way review signal
-> next-dollar recommendation
```

The marketplace should pair the right dollar with the right local receptiveness.

That means the product should not only ask, "Who can host this?"

It should ask:

```text
Which community, Host, Venue, event type, market, and budget shape
is most likely to receive this Sponsor well,
based on comparable proof for organizations like this one?
```

The marketplace should also support partner-distributed supply. A consumer app, club platform, ticketing tool, event-page product, or community network may own the daily user relationship while Analog owns Sponsor funding logic, normalized proof, and attribution rails. In that case, Analog should let the partner list downstream communities into the public directory, route SEO/GEO demand back to the partner when appropriate, and report enough structured data to unlock Sponsor funds without forcing every community to operate inside Analog directly.

This becomes a later platform-as-a-service lane. Analog should not race Partiful, Posh, Luma, Flite, LineLeap, MobiusFit, Eventbrite, or similar products for the generic event or community front door. Those platforms can keep invites, ticketing, RSVPs, organizer tools, calendars, discovery, venue workflows, social fitness groups, movement posts, and audience relationships. Analog can become the embedded layer that helps their Hosts, organizers, venues, clubs, and communities access Sponsor budgets, prove outcomes, and route future opportunity.

| Partner platform type | Why they may use Analog later | Analog should provide |
|---|---|---|
| Invite and RSVP apps | Their users create social moments but may not have sponsor funding or proof infrastructure. | Public-safe community matching, Sponsor opportunity routing, funding applications, and proof packets. |
| Ticketing and event management platforms | They own ticket sales and operations, but Sponsors still need trustworthy allocation, spend governance, and renewal proof. | Sponsor campaign rails, normalized attribution events, proof reporting, and partner API workflows. |
| IRL discovery marketplaces | They know where people are gathering, but may not want to build a full Sponsor marketplace. | Directory ingestion, canonical community matching, Sponsor demand routing, and attribution rails. |
| Movement and social fitness apps | They know which groups, rituals, and everyday movement communities are active, but may not want to build Sponsor funding rails. | Wellness fund routing, permissioned activity or group proof, Host funding applications, and Sponsor-safe recap packets. |
| Club, venue, creator, and community platforms | They own niche supply and daily engagement, but need new revenue or funding paths for organizers. | Embedded sponsorship workflows, opportunity preferences, controlled funding paths, and proof standards. |

Store the examples as a target platform category, not as confirmed partners: Partiful, Posh, Luma, Flite, LineLeap, MobiusFit, Eventbrite, and other event-page, ticketing, social experience, venue, nightlife, movement, club, creator, or community platforms.

## Marketplace Job

| Marketplace job | What the platform should learn |
|---|---|
| Match dollars | Which budget size, spend category, and Sponsor role fits a Host, Venue, community, or event type. |
| Read receptiveness | Whether the community is likely to welcome, ignore, reject, or amplify the Sponsor. |
| Benchmark fit | How similar activations performed for Sponsors in the same lane, category, market, or proof need. |
| Learn partnership quality | Whether Hosts and Sponsors are clear, responsive, respectful, and worth repeating together. |
| Reinforce good actors | Which Hosts and Sponsors should receive better matches, larger opportunities, and public-safe trust signals because proof and reviews show they improve the network. |
| Constrain bad actors | Which Hosts or Sponsors should be paused, remediated, restricted, or removed because proof and reviews show repeated trust damage. |
| Choose visibility | Whether the campaign should stay private, be broadly marketed, or be targeted to ideal ICP supply. |
| Sense demand timing | Whether a future-dated campaign gets enough qualified response in the first 48 hours to justify accepting applicants, expanding the fund, narrowing the brief, or holding. |
| Govern spend | Whether the selected Hosts can use money cleanly inside approved rules. |
| Simplify vendor records | Whether Analog can absorb the recurring payee, vendor, W-9, and 1099 support burden that makes distributed IRL spend painful. |
| Recommend next dollar | Which Host, Venue, city, event type, or campaign shape deserves renewal, expansion, or stop. |

This is AI-assisted and human-reviewed. AI can rank, compare, explain, and flag. Operators and Sponsors still decide sensitive fit, budget, compliance, and community-trust calls.

Marketplace standing should be dynamic. Good actors should become easier to fund or work with because proof and reviews reinforce them. Bad actors should become harder to route into campaigns, and severe or repeated issues should remove them from eligible matching after human review and dispute handling.

## Agentic Marketplace Experience

The Sponsor-side marketplace should feel like a guided agentic session, not a static list.

A Sponsor should be able to arrive with a rough goal:

```text
I want to fund founder dinners in SF.
I need more wellness Hosts in NYC.
Show me which applicants are worth accepting.
Tell me why this campaign should renew.
```

The product can respond through chat, simple buttons, filtered cards, generated forms, or dynamic settings. The mode matters less than the job: understand intent, reduce steps, and keep moving.

The interaction rule:

| Sponsor asks | The product should render |
|---|---|
| What should I do? | Next best action, short reason, and one clear control. |
| Who fits? | Ranked Hosts, Venues, communities, or event types with match reasons. |
| Why this match? | Receptiveness signals, proof history, market context, and risk flags. |
| What if I go deeper? | More filters, Event set options, budget shapes, proof benchmarks, files/docs, or Market Agent context. |
| What is sensitive? | Approval gates before outreach, private data sharing, spend, contracts, public claims, or final funding decisions. |

Advanced controls should appear when the workflow needs them. A basic campaign should not start with every setting visible. A complex public fund, political action program, high-ticket allocation, or vendor-heavy campaign should reveal the extra controls as soon as the Sponsor's intent makes them relevant.

## Matching Inputs

| Input | Examples | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Sponsor category | eComm/CPG, SaaS/tech, civic/nonprofit, political action, local retail, hospitality, climate, wellness | Sets proof language, risk level, and category benchmarks. |
| Sponsor goal | brand growth, mission growth, trial, account access, local legitimacy, vendor support, public-good participation | Determines what "worked" means. |
| Budget shape | micro grant, macro grant, services/tools, Venue reward, market fund, community collective fund, public program, high-ticket allocation | Determines which Hosts can use the dollar well. |
| Contributor context | fund leader, co-Sponsors, local business contributors, payment-link or invoice source, restrictions, in-kind value | Shows who backed a collective fund and what proof or restrictions must be honored. |
| Market and ICP | city, neighborhood, audience, account type, shopper type, community family, buyer persona | Narrows the supply layer to the Sponsor's best-fit surface. |
| Recursive memory and market context | city tactics guides, market culture notes, domain playbooks, Market Agent snapshots, public news, public grants, public-safe opportunity data | Keeps matching from feeling generic and helps AI explain local timing, category fit, and confidence. |
| Host and community history | cadence, attendance, proof, prior Sponsors, social response, field notes, community norms | Shows whether the Host has trust and repeatability. |
| Funding history | checks written by the Sponsor, checks received by the Host, funding reliability, renewal/increase history, permitted amount ranges | Creates trust and comfort by showing that both sides have completed funded relationships before. |
| Venue and location context | Venue mentions, space type, neighborhood, audience, accessibility, operational constraints | Shows whether the space is part of the value chain. |
| Vendor and payee context | Host, Venue, contractor, vendor, operator, payment rail, tax-document status, duplicate record risk | Shows whether Analog needs to simplify vendor records or support 1099 workflows for the campaign. |
| Two-way review history | Sponsor reviews of Hosts, Host reviews of Sponsors, 90-day checkpoint notes, closeout recommendations, disputes | Shows whether a partner is dependable, clear, respectful, low-friction, and worth routing into another collab. |
| Event type | dinner, run, workshop, farmers market, salon, class, cultural event, public-space activation | Helps benchmark against comparable moments. |
| Receptiveness signals | sentiment, prior Sponsor fit, Host note, category adjacency, opt-ins, claims, organic mentions, exclusions | Predicts whether Sponsor participation will feel additive. |
| Proof requirement | attendance, mentions, claims, sales-adjacent signal, qualified accounts, public-safe outcomes, compliance-safe field proof | Determines what must be captured before renewal. |

The match is only as useful as the reason behind it. The platform should explain why a Host, Venue, event type, or market is recommended.

A strong match reason can include capital history:

```text
This Sponsor has funded 12 Host collabs through Analog.
This Host has received checks from three Sponsors,
completed proof on time,
and earned one increased allocation.
```

Use exact dollar amounts only when permissioned. Otherwise use ranges, counts, and verified summaries.

## Social Receptiveness

Social receptiveness is the product's read on whether a community is likely to welcome a Sponsor's presence.

It should be derived from permissioned and provenance-aware signals, not from raw private surveillance.

| Signal | What it can indicate |
|---|---|
| Host note | Whether the Sponsor would improve or weaken the room. |
| Community cadence | Whether the gathering is real enough to support Sponsor dollars. |
| Prior proof | Whether similar activations created attendance, claims, mentions, or sentiment. |
| Two-way reviews | Whether prior Sponsors and Hosts found the relationship clean, clear, responsive, and worth repeating. |
| Host mentions | Whether the Host can create visible signal around the moment. |
| Venue mentions | Whether the space amplifies the event or matters to the proof chain. |
| Category adjacency | Whether the Sponsor's product, mission, or audience belongs near the ritual. |
| Audience fit | Whether the community resembles the Sponsor's target customer, account, citizen, donor, volunteer, shopper, or partner. |
| Exclusion rules | Which Sponsors, categories, or tactics the Host or community should not accept. |

The platform should produce practical labels, not magic scores:

| Label | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Strong native fit | Sponsor role is obvious and likely additive. |
| Needs careful framing | Sponsor may fit if the offer is generous and the Host controls norms. |
| Proof gap | The Host may be real, but Analog needs more attendance, mention, or field proof before funding. |
| Venue-dependent | The space is central enough that Venue authorization or Venue reward may be needed. |
| Risky fit | Sponsor category, ask, audience, or proof burden may harm trust. |

## Category Benchmarks

Sponsors should understand performance relative to organizations like them.

The benchmark should be category-aware:

| Sponsor category | Benchmark examples |
|---|---|
| eComm/CPG | product trial per attendee, claims, shopper sentiment, category mentions, repeat participation, content quality, sales-adjacent lift where available |
| SaaS/Tech | qualified attendees, account fit, follow-up meetings, partner/customer learning, adoption moments, strategic room quality |
| Civic/Public-Non-profit | access created, eligible participation, local legitimacy, public-space social impact, public-safe reporting, fraud/waste/abuse cleanliness, mission fit |
| Political Action | compliant spend, field participation where permitted, volunteer/action proof, national-to-ward geography learning, privacy-safe local sentiment |
| Local retail/hospitality | neighborhood foot traffic proxies, repeat local engagement, merchant fit, Venue mentions, local sentiment |

A strong marketplace proof sentence should sound like:

```text
For CPG Sponsors in neighborhood food markets,
this activation performed above benchmark on vendor participation,
shopper claims, and positive Host/Venue mentions.
```

or:

```text
For SaaS Sponsors targeting founder operators,
this dinner produced stronger-than-benchmark account fit
and lower Sponsor labor than a self-run event.
```

Do not overclaim direct revenue when the proof is not there. The first benchmark can be confidence, fit, spend stewardship, social receptiveness, partnership review quality, and renewal readiness.

## Campaign Visibility Modes

Every Sponsor campaign should choose a visibility mode before the ask enters the market.

| Mode | Use when | Product behavior | Risk to control |
|---|---|---|---|
| Private link-only | Sponsor has known Hosts, sensitive budget, private audience, compliance concern, or early test. | Application or campaign link works only for invited recipients or anyone with the link. Supply comes from direct invite, warm referrals, or one-to-one prospecting. | Do not imply public availability. Track source and invitation context. |
| Broadly marketed | Sponsor wants a public fund, raffle, grant, or city/category signal that pulls supply forward. | Campaign can be promoted through media, newsletters, regional dashboards, partners, owned IRL, and shareable fund pages. | Keep eligibility, budget range, allowed spend, and proof rules clear. Avoid "free money" without constraints. |
| ICP-targeted | Sponsor wants the ideal Host, community, Venue, or audience profile, not the whole market. | AI and operators route the campaign to matching catalog records, event sets, city lead lists, lookalike Hosts, partner audiences, and prospecting lanes. | Do not over-narrow too early. Explain why each target matches the Sponsor's category benchmark. |

Visibility can change over time.

```text
private pilot
-> targeted ICP expansion
-> broadly marketed fund
-> renewal against benchmarked proof
```

or:

```text
broad fund drop
-> identify strongest supply pockets
-> target the next allocation to best-fit Hosts and Venues
```

## Future-Dated Campaigns

Future-dated campaigns let Sponsors post a real ask before the activation window starts.

Use them when the Sponsor wants to know whether a market, Host type, Venue graph, or community category has enough pull before committing heavy operating work.

| Product behavior | Marketplace reason |
|---|---|
| Show the campaign start date on the application page. | Hosts know when review, funding, outreach, or activation begins. |
| Let applications open before the start date. | Sponsors can collect intent before they accept anyone. |
| Read the first 48 hours of response. | Early applicant volume, fit, source, and geography reveal whether the market is alive. |
| Keep the Sponsor free until first applicant accepted. | The Sponsor sees value before payment friction. |
| Convert at acceptance into a paid Sponsor plan, credit usage, campaign commitment, or program fee. | The revenue moment matches the operational moment. |
| Blur or limit applicants after the first five on free. | Dense supply becomes visible enough to drive PLG without giving away the full supply graph. |

The campaign should never look abandoned. If review starts later, say that plainly in the application experience.

## Public Discovery And Custom Workflows

The marketplace should be discoverable without becoming a careless directory. Analog's default should be that public-safe opportunity records can be found and used in custom workflows to create funding, partnership, sponsorship, grant, media, or Sponsor-sourcing opportunities.

Examples:

- A Sponsor asks the in-platform Analog agent to find Hosts that fit a new fund.
- A Host on an eligible future premium plan asks Analog to source potential Sponsors, forecast likely budget, or package a controlled Sponsor placement auction using its public-safe record.
- A partner platform queries Analog APIs for sponsorship opportunities that fit its communities.
- A small event-page platform or a larger consumer app lists downstream communities into Analog's directory, sends attribution events through an approved pixel or API, and unlocks Sponsor funds while keeping its own app experience.
- A city operator uses Agent MCP to build a prospecting packet for a fund or Request for Events.

The rule is:

```text
Expose opportunity data by default.
Protect private, sensitive, attendee, financial, partner-private,
public-program, and political data by design.
```

Users should see this expectation before relying on Analog as their funding record. They should also be able to opt out, correct facts, restrict fields, or move a campaign into private/link-only/compliance-sensitive visibility.

## Pre-Commitment Directory Boundary

For the NYC pre-seed Sponsor cohort, the directory is a confidence surface, not a free sourcing deliverable.

Use [NYC Pre-Seed Sponsor Cohort](/strategy/nyc-preseed-sponsor-cohort) for the current operating rule:

```text
public-safe browse
-> Sponsor believes the NYC market is real
-> Sponsor signs LOI, names budget intent, and places refundable deposit
-> Analog uses high-context matchmaking
```

Before commitment, Sponsors can see public-safe categories, sample records, aggregate density, readiness summaries, and proof tiers. They should not receive curated lists, private Host notes, raw contacts, attendee data, or bespoke recommendations.

After commitment, Analog can build Sponsor-specific Event sets, match reasons, outreach paths, and proof plans. This protects community trust and makes clear that Sponsors are paying up through deployed budget, not extracting the map for free.

## Vendor Records And 1099 Burden

A real IRL marketplace is not only a matching layer. It is also an administrative cleanup layer for the people and businesses behind the activation.

Sponsors should not have to chase scattered W-9s, duplicate vendor records, Venue payment details, contractor notes, tax-document status, or year-end reporting support every time they fund a dinner, market, run club, artist, photographer, Venue, or local operator.

Analog's direction is to become the system of record for the campaign's payee and vendor context, then take on the 1099 support burden as needed through approved payment, tax, and compliance workflows.

| Burden | Off Analog | On Analog |
|---|---|---|
| Vendor records | Each Sponsor, Host, agency, or operator keeps its own messy list. | Analog keeps one reusable vendor/payee record with source, campaign context, role, files, payment rail, and proof links. |
| W-9 and tax-document collection | Sponsors chase sensitive tax details in emails and spreadsheets. | Analog collects or routes tax-document requests through secure workflows when the campaign requires it. |
| 1099 support | Year-end reporting becomes a surprise operational tax project. | Analog tracks reportable-payment context and supports exports, partner filing, or filing workflows where required. |
| Duplicate payees | The same Venue, photographer, operator, or Host appears under many names. | Analog dedupes records and preserves which campaign, entity, and payment context created the relationship. |
| Sponsor confidence | The Sponsor sees a pile of vendors but no clean operating memory. | The Sponsor sees that Analog can govern the money and reduce the back-office burden of running IRL programs. |

This is a product direction, not a claim that the current MVP automatically files tax forms. Production workflows need secure tax-document collection, permissioning, audit logs, payment-rail awareness, counsel review, and approved tax/payment partners.

## Matching Loop

| Step | What happens | Output |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Define Sponsor thesis | Sponsor category, goal, budget, market, audience, acceptable presence, and proof need are captured. | Campaign and visibility mode. |
| 2. Build demand lanes | Event sets translate the thesis into markets, ICPs, event types, or Host categories. | Searchable/prospectable lanes. |
| 3. Score receptiveness | AI and operators compare Host, community, Venue, event type, and category signals. | Ranked candidates with reasons and risks. |
| 4. Route the ask | Campaign is private link-only, broadly marketed, or ICP-targeted based on strategy. | Applications, invites, prospecting replies, referrals. |
| 5. Fund and govern | Approved Hosts receive controlled allocations, spend rules, tasks, proof requirements, and vendor/payee record requirements where needed. | Collabs, spend cards, files, tasks, vendor/payee records. |
| 6. Benchmark proof | Spend, attendance, mentions, claims, Host/Venue ratios, sentiment, and outcomes are compared to category expectations. | Sponsor-ready proof summary. |
| 7. Capture two-way review | Sponsor and Host review responsiveness, spend stewardship, clarity, respect, proof quality, admin burden, and future fit. | Review summary and partnership-quality signal. |
| 8. Update standing | Proof and reviews reinforce good actors, flag bad actors, and update matching constraints. | Badges, reason codes, risk flags, approval gates, remediation, or removal. |
| 9. Recommend next dollar | Analog recommends renew, increase, reallocate, target more supply, broaden visibility, or stop. | Renewal or unlock decision. |

## Product Boundary

This is the direction of the marketplace layer. It should not be described as fully automated current behavior.

Active `cc-analog` approximations:

- public directory listings, categories, lists, and search
- claim and organization-owned listing workflows
- reviews, bookmarks, media, links, story, upcoming events, and editorial sponsor-fit fields
- volunteer roles, founding grant applications, and Host readiness signals
- search conversation intent, filters, history, and public-safe listing DTOs

Preserved full-platform approximations:

- campaign mission, market persona, event sets, and application portals
- managed outreach and event set prospecting
- Event Prospecting Catalog records
- Host opportunities, branded hosted event pages, files/docs, and proof packets
- Sponsor AI assistant, Sponsor data summaries, campaign content, story mentions, spend transactions, and manual closeout notes

Future product work should add:

- dynamic Sponsor session UI that infers or asks intent and reveals next actions, controls, and drill-down views progressively
- explicit campaign visibility mode
- public-safe opportunity visibility settings and opt-out controls
- production public opportunity API and Agent MCP access with scopes, audit logs, and approval gates
- AI-assisted Host, Venue, community, and event-type recommendations with reason codes
- category-specific benchmark libraries
- social receptiveness labels
- audience-fit and lookalike event recommendations
- vendor/payee records with W-9 status, duplicate detection, payment context, and 1099 support workflow flags
- two-way review objects with 90-day and closeout triggers, review summaries, visibility controls, dispute handling, and matching reason codes
- clearer Sponsor controls for private, broadly marketed, and ICP-targeted campaigns
- renewal recommendations that compare each activation to the Sponsor's category and market

The standard:

```text
Do not sell generic inventory.
Route Sponsor dollars toward receptive communities,
prove the match,
and make the next allocation smarter.
```
