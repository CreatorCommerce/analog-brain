The Event Prospecting Catalog is Analog's Apollo-like market map for IRL supply. It is not a public calendar and not a static sponsor directory. It is the claimable, provenance-aware data layer that helps sponsors find where their market already gathers and helps hosts become sponsor-ready.

The seed deck frames fit as how cleanly Analog's proof loop applies: spend, social proof, sentiment, and category lift.

## What Belongs In The Catalog

| Record type | Examples |
|---|---|
| Events | Runs, dinners, classes, pop-ups, workshops, markets, competitions, salons |
| Communities | Clubs, group chats, newsletters, recurring rituals, membership groups |
| Venues and locations | Cafes, gyms, studios, bowling alleys, restaurants, coworking spaces, hotels, entertainment spaces |
| Operators | Producers, local operators, service providers, dot connectors, city leads |
| Physical activation surfaces | Discount claims, sample pickup points, sponsored access benefits, QR/print surfaces |
| Media surfaces | Interviews, field notes, social clips, event footage, newsletter profiles, print features, documentary subjects |

## Community Universe Map

Use these families as the first taxonomy for catalog exploration. The ratings should evolve with real pilots.

| Family | Strong examples | Why it can work |
|---|---|---|
| Fitness and movement | Run clubs, walk clubs, pickleball, hiking, cycling, yoga, cold plunge | Dense supply, recurring rituals, clear sponsor demand, obvious use of funds. |
| Nature and outdoors | Birding, foraging, gardening, native plants, trail maintenance | Strong local identity and civic-friendly sponsor surfaces. |
| Civic and service | Trash cleanup, beach cleanup, mutual aid, neighborhood volunteering | Clear public benefit and strong fit for civic, public, and non-profit sponsors. |
| Maker and craft | Knitting, ceramics, vintage sourcing, repair cafes, woodworking | Durable communities with material costs and sponsor-safe rituals. |
| Visual arts | Painting, sketching, photo walks, life drawing, zines | Content-rich proof and strong local-cultural value. |
| Food and hospitality | Dinner clubs, sober socials, brunch, coffee, cookbook clubs | Obvious spend categories and venue/brand partnerships. |
| Books and learning | Book clubs, silent reading, writing, language, science cafes | Repeatable local rituals with light operational costs. |
| Games and tabletop | TTRPG, chess, board games, trivia, TCG groups | Sticky, venue-based, high-retention communities. |
| Professional and B2B | Founder clubs, AI meetups, PM/design/dev groups, affinity groups | Strong fit for tech, SaaS, VC, and recruiting-adjacent sponsors. |
| Identity and belonging | Women's social clubs, new-to-city groups, sober social, men's circles | High trust and social value; requires careful brand fit. |
| Music and performance | Choirs, dance, open mic, DJ collectives, karaoke | Cultural relevance with venue and recurring-host requirements. |
| Pets and lifestyle | Dog walking clubs, breed meetups, pet wellness gatherings | Brand-friendly, emotional, and adoption-oriented. |

Running and walking should be treated as the first clean vertical because the deck identifies dense community supply, clear brand demand, recurring weekly rituals, and obvious use of funds.

## Required Fields

Every useful catalog record should eventually capture:

- name and record type
- city, neighborhood, and operating geography
- category and audience hypothesis
- cadence and seasonality
- estimated attendance or participation range
- sponsor surfaces
- data-readiness state
- attribution-readiness state
- sponsor preferences and exclusions
- price or budget range if known
- owner/operator identity
- claim status
- provenance for each important field
- latest dialog or activation status
- media opportunity and permission state
- owned IRL follow-up potential

## Provenance

Catalog trust depends on knowing where facts came from.

Use these provenance labels:

| Provenance | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Publicly discovered | Found from web, social, directories, calendars, or manual research. |
| Brand added | Added by a sponsor during prospecting. |
| Organizer claimed | Corrected or confirmed by the host/operator. |
| Grant application | Supplied through grant, RFE, RFC, or hosted-form intake. |
| Partner API | Supplied by a community, ticketing, creator, venue, or club platform. |
| Analog verified | Checked by an operator or city lead. |
| Post-event derived | Learned from proof, report, attendance, spend, or recap. |
| Media captured | Learned from city lead interview, field footage, `The Connector`, social, YouTube, print, or owned Analog IRL. |

## Sponsor Workflow

1. Define audience, geography, category, budget, outcome, and proof requirement.
2. Search or ask an operator/agent for matching events, communities, venues, or location activations.
3. Review fit score, audience hypothesis, sponsor surfaces, data readiness, expected cost, and missing requirements.
4. Save prospects into an event set, Request for Events, or Request for Communities.
5. Add missing known events manually.
6. Ask Analog to enrich, prospect, start dialog, or create a hosted form.
7. Convert the record into an application, collab, event thread, attribution-only partner flow, media story, owned gathering, or sponsorship brief.

## Host Or Organizer Workflow

1. Claim the profile.
2. Verify ownership or operator relationship.
3. Correct category, location, cadence, audience, attendance, and sponsor surfaces.
4. Add sponsor preferences, exclusions, pricing expectations, and trust constraints.
5. Upload or connect proof.
6. Opt in to brand proposals, product support, microgrants, Requests for Events, or Requests for Communities.
7. Accept, counter, decline, or create a Request for Sponsorship.

## Privacy And Trust

Catalog profiles can be data-rich without exposing private data.

- Keep raw attendee emails and phone numbers out of sponsor search.
- Show sponsor-safe summaries, readiness states, audience hypotheses, and proof tiers.
- Store consent source for imported or hosted audience data.
- Let organizers control sponsor preferences and acceptable brand presence.
- Use the catalog to route opportunity, not to scrape and expose communities.

## Practical Use

Use the catalog when a sponsor says:

- "Find me more run clubs like this."
- "Who can host a founder breakfast in Austin?"
- "Which bowling or studio locations could run this claim flow?"
- "What events are off-platform but worth prospecting?"
- "Where do we have sponsor demand but not enough host supply?"
