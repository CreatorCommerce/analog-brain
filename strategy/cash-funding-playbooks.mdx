Read this after [MAC Versus CAC Contract](/strategy/supply-side-mac-community-champion). MAC explains how Analog models early market acquisition before unit CAC is useful. This page explains what money and support actually do for Hosts.

The MAC page is the newer anchor for supply strategy. Use it for the cost model, replacement ratio, market-stage ladder, and why Sponsor/public/partner money should replace company-funded MAC over time. Use this page for the funding mechanics: what form of money or support goes to Hosts, what it unlocks, and what proof should come back.

This is not influencer gifting. Gifting is a different market: send product widely, hope a creator posts, and back-solve direct response. Analog's market is governed Host enablement that helps a Host accomplish a real community goal: better gatherings, lower-friction access, stronger rituals, more reliable operators, and proof that tells a Sponsor or funder where the next dollar should go.

```text
Sponsor or public budget
-> cash or in-kind Host enablement
-> better gathering or recurring ritual
-> spend proof + participation + sentiment
-> renewal, larger grant, or playbook
```

Grants and raffles are the sharpest customer-funded supply hook. The point is not to give money away loosely. The point is to make Sponsor demand visible enough that qualified Hosts, spaces, and connectors self-select.

Examples:

- "Apply for up to USD 2K from the Crocs Run Recovery Fund."
- "Treat your founder friends to a night out through the Tapcart Superconnector Fund."
- "Win USD 500 for your chess club from Netflix's Queen's Gambit Fund."

Each example should route to the same product loop: campaign, event set, application, collab, governed spend, proof, and next-dollar decision.

## The Shift

Run clubs, farmers markets, dinners, pet-parent rituals, founder rooms, art walks, cleanup groups, reading clubs, chess clubs, and civic gatherings are all examples. None should become the whole thesis.

The repeatable product object is a funding playbook:

| Ingredient | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Host goal | What the Host is trying to make better, cheaper, more accessible, or more repeatable. |
| Cash budget | Micro grant, macro grant, controlled allocation, vendor budget, or operator retainer. |
| Approved spend | Hospitality, supplies, rentals, space, speakers, design, print, proof, access, or operator time. |
| Services/tools | Insurance, docs, local designers, vendors, print, space help, proof kits, or contracts. |
| Proof | Receipts, attendance, Host notes, sentiment, content where appropriate, recurrence, and next-dollar recommendation. |

Product, samples, and merch can support a collab. They should complement cash, not replace the Host budget.

Grants can also be in-kind: photo/video, print, insurance, venue help, designer support, proof kits, QR assets, or grant application support. Use the form that removes the real blocker with the least overhead.

Government and public grant discovery is one funding form inside the broader free-money motion. If a Host keeps their Analog application current, Analog can help surface relevant grants, package proof, draft application materials, and support submission with Host authorization. That same file should later help the Host win private grants, community collective funds, brand funds, or Sponsor money on-platform.

The foundational free tool is one Analog application that can route into many funding surfaces. The regional dashboard should show grants and funds a Host can one-click apply to, ask any grant-specific follow-up questions in a guided flow, and let background Agent MCP workflows prepare the packet for Host approval.

Do not make this page the grant desk. Use [Public And Nonprofit Funding Infrastructure](/strategy/public-nonprofit-funding-infrastructure) for public-fund doctrine and [NYC And SF Grant Money Map](/strategy/nyc-sf-grant-money-map) for tactical grant research.

## Corporate Grant And Support Patterns

The [Lululemon Gives Community Wellbeing Grant](https://grants.lululemon.com/s/?language=en_US#time), its [Goodstack registration flow](https://nomination.goodstack.org/grantprogram_000000DKZzCtSKvDhLm0PibsLJIhM), and the [Nike Community Impact Fund](https://about.nike.com/en/mission/initiatives/nike-community-impact-fund) are useful market signals for how corporate Sponsors think about support.

Lululemon shows the registration-first corporate grant pattern:

```text
public fund story
-> registration form
-> eligibility and verification
-> invitation to apply
-> application review
-> grant award
-> reporting and renewal story
```

Lululemon frames the program around community-led charitable organizations advancing wellbeing through movement. The 2027 cycle opens applications from July 6 to July 31, 2026, with registration due July 24, 2026 at 5:00pm PT. After registration, organizations are told to expect an eligibility status email and, if eligible, an invitation to apply, typically within 72 hours.

Goodstack powers the registration layer. Its form pattern is practical:

| Corporate grant field | What it tells Analog |
|---|---|
| Organization search by country, name, or registry ID | Corporate Sponsors want verified entities before deeper review. Analog should keep a reusable Host funding record instead of making each fund start from zero. |
| Fallback for organizations not found in the registry | Strong local supply may be real before it is cleanly indexed. Analog needs an add-and-verify path for Hosts, fiscal sponsors, community businesses, and informal operators. |
| Organization type and fiscal sponsor fields | Fund eligibility is often entity-dependent. Analog should capture nonprofit, fiscally sponsored, business, government, and social-impact status without assuming every good Host is a charity. |
| Operating budget and geography | Sponsors use size, country, and market rules to screen applicants. Analog should turn this into market-fit and allocation logic, not generic form admin. |
| Contact, consent, language, deadline, and email guidance | Grants are operational programs, not just landing pages. Analog should track applicant communication, status, missing docs, reminders, and approval gates. |
| Registration before full application | The best corporate flow reduces wasted applicant time and review load. Analog should support a light eligibility gate before asking for the full pitch, budget, files, and proof plan. |

Analog should not copy generic corporate grant software. It should learn the corporate mental model, then add the IRL layer that Goodstack-style intake does not own:

| Generic corporate grant need | Analog IRL spin |
|---|---|
| Verify eligible organizations. | Verify fundable Hosts, venues, fiscal sponsors, operators, and local trust surfaces. |
| Collect registration and application fields. | Reuse one Host funding record, then ask only fund-specific deltas. |
| Screen for mission fit. | Match Event sets, rituals, audiences, cities, and proof standards to the Sponsor thesis. |
| Award money. | Govern spend through approved categories, cards, invoices, vendors, or tranches. |
| Receive reports. | Produce proof packets: receipts, attendance, Host notes, sentiment, authorized mentions, photos where appropriate, and next-dollar recommendations. |
| Tell a corporate impact story. | Show which IRL communities, markets, Hosts, and rituals became stronger because the Sponsor funded them. |

For Analog, a Lululemon-style fund should become:

```text
Sponsor fund page
-> Host registration gate
-> eligibility and verification
-> Event set fit
-> approved budget and spend rules
-> funded IRL activation or series
-> proof packet
-> renewal, larger grant, or market playbook
```

This is especially relevant for movement, wellness, public health, access, arts, youth, sports, and local commerce funds. The Sponsor may call it a grant program. Analog should treat it as structured IRL market deployment.

Nike adds a second corporate support pattern: a global brand mission becomes local grantmaking through employee proximity, nonprofit and school eligibility, trained teammate review, regional grant partners, and a recurring application-to-disbursement cycle.

The Nike-style pattern:

```text
global support mission
-> priority employee communities and regions
-> nonprofit and school applicants
-> partner-administered application window
-> trained employee review and recommendation
-> grant disbursement
-> play, sport, and access story
```

Analog should not pretend to replace grant administrators, foundations, or fiscal sponsors. It should make the downstream support legible, governed, and renewable.

| Nike-style support move | Analog version |
|---|---|
| Global mission around kids, play, and sport. | Sponsor fund page with a clear support thesis, eligible Host types, market focus, allowed spend, and proof standard. |
| Funding limited to places where employees live and work. | Event sets by city, employee market, local circuit, school and community partner type, or play and sport access need. |
| Nonprofit and school applicants. | Verified Hosts, schools, nonprofits, fiscal sponsors, youth-sport operators, rec leagues, park stewards, community coaches, and trusted local partners. |
| Trained employee review and recommendations. | Local reviewer, city lead, advisory, or Sponsor teammate notes that explain community fit without exposing private applicant data. |
| Regional grant administrators and philanthropic partners. | Partner, fiscal sponsor, fund administrator, or nonprofit records attached in files/docs and reporting workflows. |
| Annual application and disbursement rhythm. | Campaign start dates, application windows, review queues, award dates, spend windows, and closeout/reporting deadlines. |
| Grant payments. | Controlled activation budget, spend card, invoice, vendor, tranche, or partner-administered payment rail depending on the program. |
| Access to play and sport as the impact story. | Proof packet showing access created, participation, recurring play and sport, trusted local operator, spend stewardship, photos or quotes where permitted, and next-dollar recommendation. |

What Nike adds that Lululemon does not:

| Concept | Why it matters for Analog |
|---|---|
| Employee-led local judgment | Large organizations may want their own people close to the community to help review, recommend, or contextualize support. Analog should preserve that judgment as structured notes and approval context. |
| Place-based employee communities | Corporate support may follow where teammates live and work, not only where a brand has retail, customers, or media goals. Event sets should handle employee-dense markets and regional windows. |
| Nonprofit and school channels | Youth, school, sport, and play programs need stronger eligibility, safeguarding, and partner records than a normal brand activation. |
| Intermediary fund administration | Some Sponsors will operate through foundations or grant administrators. Analog should complement that layer with Host records, spend proof, and local activation proof. |
| Application window plus disbursement date | Operators need clear timing. Start dates, review windows, and payment/disbursement expectations are part of the product, not just copy. |
| Support language before campaign language | The Sponsor may be trying to make play, sport, wellbeing, or local access possible. Analog should translate that into governed support, not force it into ad inventory. |

The [NYC Pre-Seed Sponsor Cohort](/strategy/nyc-preseed-sponsor-cohort) is the immediate selective-fund bridge. Treat it as a closed-door budget-intent motion, not a normal paid plan: Sponsors name at least USD 50k of intended NYC partnership budget, USD 100k+ is best, the commitment runs through an LOI and refundable USD 500 deposit, and Analog does not take a fee or rake from those first engagements. High-context matchmaking starts after commitment so Analog protects the social capital behind the directory.

## Funding Ladder

| Layer | Typical budget | Best for | Renewal question |
|---|---:|---|---|
| Micro Grant | USD 100 to USD 500/month | Coffee, libations, snacks, supplies, small rentals, printed signs, accessibility, light venue support. | Did a small amount of cash reduce friction and strengthen the ritual? |
| Starter Series | USD 500 to USD 1.5k/month | Monthly event support, room minimums, instructor/speaker fees, proof capture, simple design/print. | Is this a repeatable series with sponsor-safe proof? |
| Macro Grant | USD 1k to USD 5k/month | Proven organizers, strategic rooms, recurring programming, operator margin, local production. | Does this Host create outsized category, account, civic, or community value? |
| Operator Retainer | USD 3k to USD 10k+/month | Operators who function like city leads, strategic room builders, or supply engines. | Is this person becoming repeatable market infrastructure? |
| Embedded Host Treasury | Larger approved balance | Trusted recurring Hosts, arts orgs, venues, nonprofits, community businesses, or city operators who need a real operating account instead of one-off cards. | Can Analog offer Host autonomy while still giving Sponsors abstracted fraud/waste/abuse, proof, pacing, and renewal confidence? |

Keep Host enablement and Analog revenue separate. Controlled activation budget should stay visible apart from platform, admin, sourcing, support, media, or content-rights fees.

Embedded Host treasury is a future rail, not current MVP behavior. It should be built through compliant banking partners and used only when the Host has enough trust, proof, and recurring work to justify a central Analog checking-style balance.

## Services And Tools

Cash funding works better when Analog also removes operating drag.

| Service/tool | Why it matters | Product direction |
|---|---|---|
| Insurance and COI flows | Many gatherings stall on risk, coverage, venue requirements, waivers, or permits. | partner workflow, checklist, coverage status, COI request |
| Print-on-demand | Hosts need signs, QR cards, table cards, zines, posters, guides, and sponsor-safe materials. | print request, templates, vendor routing, proof photo, cost tracking |
| Documents and contracts | Sponsors, Hosts, venues, speakers, and vendors need simple scopes and obligations. | templates, signature-ready files, approvals, versioning |
| Local designer bench | Great gatherings need taste: flyers, identity, menus, maps, merch, and print layout. | provider catalog, brief template, design proof |
| Vendor/rental catalog | Food, staffing, audio, rentals, photo, security, cleaning, transport repeat across activations. | vendor profiles, pricing notes, reliability score |
| Proof kits | Hosts should not invent reporting after the event. | checklist, hosted page, QR/claim flow, receipt prompt, Host note |

Services should unblock playbooks, then become product, templates, partner workflows, contractor benches, or priced managed-support packages.

## Print And Publication Moat

Print is not a side project. It can become the supply moat.

AnalogNYC should publish the first profitable consumer and retail print product that makes the local gathering economy desirable, navigable, and status-rich. AnalogSF and later city brands should follow only when transfer-market demand or Sponsor-funded city work justifies the added operating surface.

The magazine should include upcoming events, Host profiles, dot connector profiles, city scene reports, fund drops, Sponsor perspectives when clearly labeled, local retail/eComm placements, and QR flows into event pages, applications, claims, and subscriptions.

```text
If you churn, you lose the map of what is worth showing up to.
```

Media delivered to the door has a different emotional weight than another feed post. It creates a city object people keep, share, photograph, and use to plan their month. That object can recruit Hosts, sell Sponsors, fill events, and make Analog the publication of the gathering economy.

Use [12-Month Market Focus](/strategy/twelve-month-market-focus) and [Media, Connectors, And Funding Engine](/strategy/media-owned-irl-engine) for the deeper print/media operating loop.

## Civic/Public-Non-profit Lane

Municipal, NGO, foundation, and public-good funding can become one of Analog's most important lanes if the product solves the hard trust problem: get money into honest builders' pockets quickly while reducing fraud, waste, abuse, and reporting burden.

Lead with concrete fundable problems:

- loneliness and social connection
- public space activation
- youth, arts, sports, and entrepreneurship programming
- neighborhood commercial corridor vitality
- wellness and preventive health participation
- climate, cleanup, repair, and stewardship
- safer nightlife, sober social, and third-place access
- local creator, operator, and small business income

Use [Public And Nonprofit Funding Infrastructure](/strategy/public-nonprofit-funding-infrastructure) for the canonical public/nonprofit model.

When the public-money program funds parks, public spaces, libraries, corridors, cleanups, stewardship, wellness access, youth, arts, sports, or social connection work, use [Public Space Social Impact Measurement](/references/public-space-social-impact-measurement) to decide whether the proof packet should include participant-reported belonging, connection, trust, agency, respect, meaningfulness, and cross-group contact.

When the blocker is permit readiness, insurance, site plans, weather, vending, concessions, ticketing, or public-space stewardship funding, use [Public Space Activation Infrastructure](/references/public-space-activation-infrastructure) before designing the fund.

## Political Action Lane

Political campaigns, advocacy organizations, ballot initiatives, PACs, party committees, and donor coalitions can use the same cash-funding mechanics only with tighter controls. Treat political action as a separate commercial lane because field spend, privacy, data use, and compliance rules are different from civic/public-good funding.

The clean test is a grassroots activation fund: state or campaign budget routes into county, city, Host, and event-level allocations with approved spend, receipts, files/docs, field proof, and next-allocation recommendations.

Use [Campaign Grassroots Activation Funds](/use-cases/campaign-grassroots-activation-funds) for the canonical use case.

## First Race To Market

1. Close the NYC pre-seed Sponsor cohort: LOI budget intent, refundable deposit, category fit, no fee/rake, and no curated list access before commitment.
2. Use one reusable Analog application and pitch tools to help Hosts and connectors package proof, reach, grant readiness, eligibility status, and funding asks.
3. Launch Sponsor-backed grants and raffles in AnalogNYC.
4. Use AnalogNYC media to identify, elevate, and route Hosts into those playbooks; use AnalogSF as transfer-market research or execution only when justified.
5. Research public and nonprofit grant money in priority markets and support Host-authorized applications where the fit is real.
6. Test one corporate grant-style or community-impact support flow with an enterprise Sponsor: light eligibility gate first, local review context second, full application third, funded IRL activation fourth.
7. Test one community collective fund in a dense neighborhood where 10 to 12 local contributors can back more IRL through one governed campaign.
8. Build the services/tooling layer that makes funded gatherings easier: insurance, print, docs, vendors, proof.
9. Package the best operators into larger recurring budgets and strategic rooms.
10. Build a credible public-money lane with transparent spend, eligibility, and proof.

Movement communities can be part of the first test because they are visible and repeat often. They are not the moat by themselves. The moat is the funding, proof, services, media, and selection system that can travel across every community where trusted Hosts are underfunded.

## Guardrails

- Cash does not mean unrestricted money.
- Public funding should be transparent, defensible, and permissioned.
- Do not make communities perform like influencers to justify funding.
- Do not let print become advertorial inventory that erodes trust.
- Do not build services as a hidden agency trap.
- Do not over-index on one vertical when the same funding mechanics apply across many community families.
