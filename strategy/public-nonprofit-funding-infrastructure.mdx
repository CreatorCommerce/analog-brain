Analog's public and nonprofit opportunity is the same company thesis with a different buyer language:

> Fund the people closest to participation, then make the use of that money safe, legible, and renewable.

Government agencies, foundations, NGOs, nonprofits, public/private coalitions, business improvement districts, hospital community-benefit programs, universities, and civic institutions all face the same operating problem. They may have mission capital, but the last-mile operators who can create attendance, trust, access, local legitimacy, and civic action are often small, informal, undercapitalized, or administratively overloaded.

Analog should become the funding infrastructure that lets those upstream funders move money downstream to honest community builders without losing control, speed, or public trust.

```text
Public or nonprofit mission budget
-> downstream Host, operator, space, or community partner
-> governed spend + services/tools
-> participation, access, local legitimacy, and proof
-> report, renewal, larger grant, or reallocation
```

This is not a side lane from sponsorship. It is one of the highest-stakes versions of the same product: large capital pools trying to create real-world participation through distributed humans.

## Core Thesis

Public and nonprofit funders do not only need grant software. They need an operating layer for community action.

The hardest questions are practical:

- Who is close enough to the community to create trust?
- What should they be allowed to spend on?
- How do they get money before the work happens instead of waiting for reimbursement?
- How does the funder prevent fraud, waste, abuse, conflicts, and mission drift?
- What proof is enough to release the next tranche?
- Which Hosts, neighborhoods, formats, or partners deserve deeper investment?

Analog's answer should be:

```text
Define the mission
-> open or source the right downstream supply
-> govern the money
-> support execution
-> monitor risk
-> turn messy local work into credible reporting
-> decide the next allocation
```

The strategic wedge is not "Analog runs civic events." The wedge is:

> Analog helps public and nonprofit funders safely deploy serious capital into community-led civic action.

## Downstream Hosts

Public and nonprofit programs should not assume the best operator is always a large institution.

The downstream Host can be:

- a neighborhood organizer
- a small business owner
- a community founder
- a youth, arts, sports, wellness, climate, or civic group
- a faith or cultural organization
- a public-space steward
- a venue, cafe, studio, library partner, or local space owner
- a dot connector who can assemble the right room
- a local designer, producer, photographer, print vendor, insurance navigator, or operator
- an existing nonprofit subchapter, volunteer leader, ambassador, fellow, or grantee

The product should make these actors fundable without pretending all of them have the same administrative capacity.

## How Analog Helps Nonprofits

Nonprofits often know the mission, but they do not always have the hyperlocal reach, cultural fluency, or operating bandwidth to activate every community directly.

Analog should help nonprofits accomplish goals by funding downstream communities.

| Nonprofit need | Downstream funding answer | Analog product direction |
|---|---|---|
| Reach people the nonprofit cannot reliably reach alone. | Fund trusted Hosts, local groups, small businesses, and community leaders already close to the audience. | Event Prospecting Catalog, applications, city lead referrals, host profiles, and local legitimacy notes. |
| Turn a broad mission into real gatherings. | Convert the mission into funded rituals, workshops, access moments, service days, local dinners, clubs, or recurring series. | Campaigns, event sets, fund pages, Host events, tasks, and proof templates. |
| Reduce administrative load on small partners. | Give partners controlled spend, clear scopes, approved categories, and lightweight proof instead of reimbursement chaos. | Collabs, spend cards, files/docs, required todos, receipt prompts, and Host notes. |
| Prove value to donors, boards, and grantors. | Package spend stewardship, participation, stories, local legitimacy, access, and lessons learned. | Sponsor data, proof packets, AI-assisted report drafts, board-ready summaries, and renewal memos. |
| Build durable local capacity. | Identify the best downstream operators and graduate them into macro grants, operator retainers, or fellowships. | Unlock scoring, proof history, operator profiles, allocation models, and tranche gates. |

The nonprofit pitch is not "outsource your mission." It is:

> Keep the mission, governance, and accountability upstream. Fund the trusted community edge where participation actually happens.

## Program Flow

<Steps>
  <Step title="Define the mission and fund lane">
    Name the outcome area, geography, eligible Hosts, allowed uses, restricted uses, proof standard, privacy boundaries, and renewal rule.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Create or source downstream supply">
    Use city leads, media, partners, application portals, Event Prospecting Catalog records, nonprofit chapters, and public referrals to find qualified Hosts.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Approve with support needs visible">
    Do not only ask for a budget. Let Hosts request space, insurance, print, local design, vendors, materials, translation, accessibility, or proof support.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Deploy the right rail">
    Use spend cards for small governed budgets, invoices/contracts for larger providers, and files/docs for scopes, approvals, insurance, vendor records, and public constraints.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Monitor spend, risk, and proof">
    Watch receipts, merchant categories, timing, anomalies, duplicate vendors, missing proof, public sensitivity, community sentiment, and task completion.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Report and decide the next allocation">
    Generate funder-ready summaries, public-safe reports, board updates, tranche recommendations, renewal memos, and reallocation notes.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## AI Depth: Public Trust And Grant Safety

AI should make public and nonprofit capital feel safer to deploy without pretending the system can replace human approval.

| AI surface | What it should produce |
|---|---|
| Mission-to-playbook translation | Recommended fund lanes, eligible activities, spend categories, proof standards, and Host archetypes. |
| Eligibility review | Checks against geography, organization type, program goals, conflicts, required documents, and restricted uses. |
| Host and partner diligence | Prior proof, local legitimacy, operational capacity, public risk, insurance needs, community fit, and support needs. |
| Budget reasonableness | Flags unusually high line items, duplicate requests, vague budgets, vendor concentration, missing quotes, or mismatch to program scope. |
| Fraud/waste/abuse review | Duplicate vendors, suspicious timing, weak receipts, merchant-category mismatch, repeated anomalies, conflicts, and missing proof. |
| Allowable-use QA | Compares spend and invoices against approved categories, program rules, grant restrictions, and public-reporting commitments. |
| Equity and geography scan | Shows distribution by neighborhood, audience, access type, Host type, and underserved areas where appropriate. |
| Public-sensitivity monitor | Flags brand safety, political sensitivity, community backlash, youth/sensitive-audience issues, and public narrative risks. |
| Proof synthesis | Turns receipts, Host notes, attendance, photos where appropriate, field notes, sentiment, and files into funder-ready summaries. |
| Tranche recommendation | Release, hold, reallocate, increase support, request documentation, narrow eligibility, or stop. |
| Grant and board reporting draft | Creates a structured report draft with spend, participation, outcomes, lessons, risks, and next-dollar recommendations. |

The AI posture should be conservative: assistant, analyst, and reviewer. Not autonomous grantmaker.

## Proof Standards

Public and nonprofit proof should be simple enough for Hosts to complete and credible enough for funders to defend.

| Proof lane | What it answers |
|---|---|
| Spend stewardship | Was money used for approved community work, with receipts, vendor notes, unused-fund visibility, and anomaly review? |
| Access created | Did the funding make participation easier, cheaper, more available, more local, or more welcoming? |
| Participation | Who showed up in privacy-safe aggregate terms, how often, and through what source? |
| Local legitimacy | Did the Host have trust, community permission, and relevant relationships? |
| Recurrence | Did the funding create or strengthen a repeatable ritual instead of a one-off press moment? |
| Mission fit | Did the activity map to the funder's stated goal without overclaiming long-term outcomes? |
| Community sentiment | Did the funded presence feel additive, respectful, and useful to the community? |
| Capacity built | Did the Host, group, or small partner become more capable for the next activation? |
| Public reportability | Can the funder explain what happened without exposing private attendee data or fragile community context? |

Outcome claims should stay disciplined. Analog can report participation, access, recurrence, spend, sentiment, and field evidence early. Longer-term health, safety, economic, civic, or social outcomes require stronger evaluation partners and should not be overpromised.

## Spend Rails

The spend card remains useful, but major public and nonprofit programs need more than cards.

| Spend type | Good rail | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| Small Host budget | Spend card with category, amount, expiration, and event/fund context. | Best for coffee, supplies, print, materials, local vendors, room minimums, and event-scoped costs. |
| Vendor or service provider | Invoice, contract, scope, approval note, and deliverable proof. | Use for insurance, production, design, printing, space, accessibility, translation, safety, or professional services. |
| Larger community partner | Contract, milestone, proof gate, and tranche plan. | Use when the partner is effectively running a program lane, not only one event. |
| Public/private pooled fund | Allocation model, fund rules, governance docs, risk register, and reporting calendar. | Keep capital sources, restrictions, fees, and pass-through budgets legible. |
| Nonprofit regranting | Application, agreement, controlled budget, eligibility docs, and report template. | Keep upstream mission control while reducing downstream admin burden. |

Do not force every public or nonprofit dollar through a card. Analog's role is governance, proof, and reporting across the right rails.

| Application | How Analog stays in the loop |

The larger the capital pool, the more Analog should sell investment safety, fund administration, operator underwriting, AI monitoring, and reporting infrastructure.

## Pricing And Revenue

Public and nonprofit pricing should be transparent enough to survive scrutiny.

Separate:

- capital deployed to Hosts, operators, communities, vendors, or public-good benefits
- Analog implementation fee
- fund administration fee
- sourcing or application management fee
- AI monitoring and reporting fee
- managed support or operator bench
- media/print/public reporting package
- pass-through vendor costs
- compliance, legal, insurance, or evaluation partner costs

Avoid hiding Analog revenue inside community money. For public buyers and many nonprofits, opaque take rates create trust risk. Use clear admin, implementation, reporting, and managed-support line items.

## Guardrails

- Do not promise public-sector outcomes the product cannot measure.
- Do not imply Analog is a legal, compliance, procurement, or grant-accounting substitute.
- Keep human approval on eligibility, sensitive funding decisions, tranches, public reports, and high-risk exceptions.
- Do not expose raw attendee data, vulnerable community context, or sensitive Host information as a public proof asset.
- Do not make small community partners carry enterprise reporting burdens.
- Do not let public or nonprofit money become extractive content capture.
- Do not frame downstream Hosts as interchangeable vendors when their trust is the asset.
- Do not call money unrestricted if it is governed by program rules, spend categories, approvals, or proof gates.

| Test | Why it is strong |

| City social-connection fund | A city or public/private coalition funds recurring local rituals that create access and belonging. |
| Corridor activation fund | Local merchants, Hosts, artists, and spaces receive governed budgets to create repeatable neighborhood programming. |
| Youth/arts/sports starter kits | Small grants plus supplies, print, space, and partner requirements for accessible local programming. |
| Climate stewardship fund | Cleanups, repair, gardening, recycling, and local resilience work with clear materials spend and action proof. |
| Public/private wellness access fund | Sponsored access to movement, sober social, health education, or preventive wellness with privacy-safe reporting. |

The test is not whether a single event feels good. The test is whether Analog can help a public or nonprofit Sponsor move money downstream faster, prove it responsibly, and know which community builders deserve the next dollar.
