Analog's public and nonprofit opportunity is the same company thesis in public-good language:

Read this after [Sponsor Personas And Pipeline](/strategy/sponsor-personas) and [Host Models And Personas](/strategy/host-models-personas). Those pages explain the commercial actors. This page explains the public-good capital lane.

> Fund the people closest to participation, then make the use of that money safe, legible, and renewable.

Government agencies, foundations, NGOs, nonprofits, public/private coalitions, BIDs, hospitals, universities, and civic institutions often have mission capital but struggle to reach the small local operators who can create trust, attendance, access, and legitimacy. Analog should become the operating layer that helps those upstream funders move money downstream without losing control, speed, or public trust.

Keep this lane distinct from political action. Civic/public/nonprofit programs fund public-good participation and mission outcomes. Political campaigns, PACs, ballot initiatives, party committees, advocacy organizations, and donor coalitions should use [Campaign Grassroots Activation Funds](/use-cases/campaign-grassroots-activation-funds) when the work involves electoral, advocacy, field, or persuasion-sensitive activity.

```text
Mission budget
-> downstream Host, operator, space, or community partner
-> governed spend + services/tools
-> participation, access, local legitimacy, and proof
-> report, renewal, larger grant, or reallocation
```

## The Wedge

Public and nonprofit funders do not only need grant software. They need a way to answer practical deployment questions:

| Question | Analog answer |
|---|---|
| Who is close enough to the community to create trust? | City leads, partner referrals, applications, media, and the Event Prospecting Catalog surface eligible Hosts and local operators. |
| What should they be allowed to spend on? | Fund lanes define geography, eligibility, approved uses, restricted uses, budget size, proof standard, and renewal rules. |
| How does money reach the edge before the work happens? | Small budgets can use spend cards; larger work can use invoices, contracts, scopes, tranches, and vendor records. |
| How does the funder reduce fraud, waste, abuse, and mission drift? | Receipts, category rules, anomaly flags, conflict checks, missing-proof prompts, human approvals, and AI-assisted reviews. |
| What proof releases the next dollar? | Spend stewardship, access created, participation, recurrence, Host notes, sentiment, public-safe summaries, and funder reports. |

The strategic wedge is not "Analog runs civic events." It is:

> Analog helps public and nonprofit Sponsors safely deploy serious capital into community-led civic action.

This is still a local-first supply problem. A nonprofit, city agency, campaign, SaaS company, and eComm brand may all need access to the same community builders in a market, but the public/nonprofit lane needs the strongest public-safe reporting, eligibility logic, and mission-governance wrapper.

Direct government grants may be slow. The first credible path can run through nonprofits, foundations, BIDs, universities, hospitals, cultural institutions, or public/private coalitions that already have mission budgets and need better downstream controls. Direct agency procurement becomes easier after Analog can show eligibility rules, allowable-use controls, receipt history, public-safe reporting, and repeatable local supply.

For federally influenced programs, keep the product vocabulary close to established controls: documented internal controls, compliance monitoring, prompt action on noncompliance, safeguards for sensitive information, and proactive fraud-risk management. See [2 CFR 200.303](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/2/200.303) and the [GAO Fraud Risk Management Framework](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-15-593sp) for the control posture, without presenting Analog as a replacement for counsel, grants administration, or agency-specific compliance.

## Grant Discovery And Auto-Apply

Public money is not only a future Sponsor lane. It is a near-term supply-capture tool.

Analog should research the grant money on the table, match it to Hosts, and help Hosts become application-ready by virtue of having an Analog record. The free pitch tool should feel like:

```text
Get your event history and proof in one place.
Analog finds grant opportunities you may qualify for.
With your authorization, Analog helps package and apply.
That same record later helps you win brand and Sponsor money.
```

This is a practical way to make "free money" real. A Host who joins Analog should get better at applying for government, foundation, nonprofit, public/private, and Sponsor-backed funds.

## OpenClaw-Style Grant Tactics

Use `OpenClaw tactics` as the operating shorthand for aggressive but permissioned grant discovery and packaging. It means Analog should systematically search, match, prepare, and track grant opportunities for Hosts. It does not mean submitting anything without Host authorization or replacing legal/grants counsel.

| Step | Analog action | Human control | Product memory created |
|---|---|---|---|
| Discover | Search public portals, foundation lists, city budgets, BID programs, cultural offices, and nonprofit calls for proposals. | Operator reviews source quality and deadline reality. | grant opportunity catalog, market grant map. |
| Match | Compare geography, Host type, audience, allowable uses, proof needs, and deadlines against Host records. | Host confirms eligibility facts and interest. | Host opportunity list, public-fund readiness score. |
| Package | Draft application answers, budget language, proof summaries, event history, photos, files, and use-of-funds narrative. | Host edits facts and approves final materials. | Analog Doc, files/docs, reusable pitch packet. |
| Apply or assist | Submit, co-submit, or prepare materials only where the Host has authorized the action and the program permits it. | Host authorization is required; counsel or grant specialist reviews sensitive programs. | application status, deadline, submitted files. |
| Track | Monitor status, follow-ups, missing materials, reporting requirements, award notices, and rejections. | Operator escalates deadlines and unusual requirements. | grant status, next step, no-go note. |
| Route and prove | If funded, structure allowable spend, services/tools, proof, reporting, and renewal or next-grant recommendations. | Funder rules, Host approval, and reporting requirements govern the workflow. | spend record, proof packet, report draft, next-dollar recommendation. |

## Sponsor And Host Shapes

Use `Sponsor` as the product actor, but name the real buyer precisely.

| Sponsor type | What they fund | Analog role |
|---|---|---|
| City/county agency or BID | social connection, corridors, public space, arts, youth, safety, local commerce | eligibility, controlled spend, local supply, public reporting |
| Foundation or donor-advised program | mission-aligned impact through local actors | regranting lanes, grantee discovery, lightweight proof, board reports |
| Nonprofit or NGO | program reach, advocacy, education, service access, member activation | downstream Host funding, partner proof, donor/grantor reporting |
| Public/private coalition | mixed civic, health, climate, cultural, or economic goals | shared governance, clear roles, spend records, proof gates |
| Hospital, university, or anchor institution | community benefit, preventive health, workforce, student/city connection | privacy-safe participation proof and local partner activation |

Downstream Hosts can be neighborhood organizers, community founders, small businesses, civic groups, public-space stewards, venues, local chapters, volunteer leaders, artists, coaches, designers, print vendors, insurance navigators, or dot connectors. The product should make these actors fundable without pretending they all have enterprise administrative capacity.

## Fund Lanes

Public and nonprofit money should ladder from small, fast, controlled grants into larger programs when proof and trust exist.

| Lane | Use | Proof standard |
|---|---|---|
| Civic Micro Grant | USD 100 to USD 500 for coffee, meals, supplies, accessibility, print, room minimums, equipment, or small local rituals. | receipt, Host note, participation, local legitimacy, renewal flag |
| Community Starter Kit | materials, templates, insurance help, print, space support, and a small operating budget | blocker removed, gathering launched, support needs visible |
| Nonprofit Regranting Lane | nonprofit/foundation funds aligned local groups | eligibility, approved budget, proof packet, donor/board summary |
| Corridor Activation Fund | merchants, artists, Hosts, spaces, foot traffic, public-space programming | merchant/Host map, spend records, participation, sentiment |
| Social Connection Fund | recurring gatherings that reduce isolation and strengthen third places | recurrence, access created, attendance, Host notes |
| Youth/Arts/Sports/Culture Fund | clubs, arts, music, rec sports, workshops, cultural programming | safeguards, partner requirements, supplies/space/instructor proof |
| Public Health/Wellness Access Fund | free or lower-cost movement, education, sober social, nutrition, screenings, or access moments | privacy-safe participation proof and cautious outcome language |
| Operator Fellowship | recurring support for the best local operators | macro grant, proof history, tranche gates, field notes |
| Public/Private City Program | large pooled capital from brands, agencies, philanthropy, and local institutions | investment memo, allocation model, risk register, reporting calendar |

Do not mix electoral objectives into public/nonprofit fund lanes. If a program touches policy advocacy, ballot activity, electioneering, or party/candidate field work, separate the Sponsor, budget, data, proof, and legal controls before any Host funding is approved.

## Nonprofit Downstream Funding

The nonprofit pitch is:

> Keep the mission, governance, and accountability upstream. Fund the trusted community edge where participation actually happens.

| Nonprofit need | Analog motion |
|---|---|
| Reach people the nonprofit cannot reliably reach alone. | Fund trusted Hosts, small businesses, local groups, and community leaders already close to the audience. |
| Turn a broad mission into real gatherings. | Convert the mission into funded rituals, workshops, access moments, service days, dinners, clubs, or recurring series. |
| Reduce admin load on small partners. | Give partners controlled spend, clear scopes, approved categories, and lightweight proof instead of reimbursement chaos. |
| Prove value to donors, boards, and grantors. | Package spend stewardship, participation, stories, local legitimacy, access, risks, and next-dollar recommendations. |
| Build durable local capacity. | Graduate strong operators into macro grants, retainers, fellowships, or deeper program roles. |

## Operating Flow

1. Define the mission, geography, eligible Hosts, allowed uses, restricted uses, proof standard, privacy boundary, and renewal rule.
2. Source downstream supply through city leads, media, partners, applications, referrals, and catalog records.
3. Approve with support needs visible: space, insurance, print, design, vendors, accessibility, translation, materials, or proof help.
4. Deploy through the right rail: spend card, invoice, contract, milestone, vendor scope, or program tranche.
5. Monitor receipts, merchant categories, timing, anomalies, conflicts, public sensitivity, sentiment, and missing proof.
6. Report spend, participation, lessons, risks, and tranche or renewal recommendations.

## AI Depth

AI should make public and nonprofit capital safer to deploy without replacing human approval.

| AI surface | Output |
|---|---|
| Mission-to-playbook translation | fund lanes, eligible activities, spend categories, proof standards, Host archetypes |
| Eligibility and diligence review | geography, documents, conflicts, prior proof, local legitimacy, operational capacity, public risk |
| Budget and allowable-use QA | reasonableness flags, duplicate requests, missing quotes, category mismatches, restricted-use issues |
| Fraud/waste/abuse review | suspicious timing, duplicate vendors, weak receipts, repeated anomalies, missing proof |
| Public sensitivity and equity scan | geographic distribution, audience access, sensitive groups, narrative risk, public-reporting caution |
| Proof and reporting synthesis | funder-ready summaries, board updates, public-safe reports, tranche recommendations |

The AI posture should be conservative: assistant, analyst, and reviewer. Not autonomous grantmaker.

## Proof And Pricing Rules

Public and nonprofit proof should show what happened without overclaiming long-term outcomes. Early proof can cover spend stewardship, access created, participation, recurrence, local legitimacy, sentiment, Host capacity, and public-safe reporting. Health, safety, economic, civic, or social outcomes need stronger evaluation partners.

Pricing should be transparent enough to survive scrutiny. Separate pass-through community money from Analog implementation, fund administration, sourcing/application management, AI monitoring/reporting, managed support, media/print/public reporting, vendor costs, and legal/compliance/evaluation partners.

Do not force every dollar through a card. Analog's role is governance, proof, reporting, and allocation confidence across the right rails.

For deeper recurring partners, a future Host treasury rail can make more sense than one-off spend cards. A nonprofit, foundation, BID, or public/private coalition could fund a larger approved balance into a trusted Host's Analog checking-style account, while Analog reports recipient-authorized fraud/waste/abuse anomalies, proof completeness, budget pacing, restricted-use risk, and renewal recommendations. This requires compliant banking partners, written terms, eligibility controls, privacy boundaries, and human review.

Any grade derived from account activity is sensitive derived financial data. Public and nonprofit programs should be especially careful because the grade may become an eligibility or tranche gate. Require recipient authorization, disclosed sponsor/funder use, objective methodology, explainability, dispute paths, safer labels, and counsel review for government, individual/consumer, repayable, credit-like, or routine third-party scoring scenarios.
