Use `Sponsor` for the product actor. In the market, a sponsor is anyone who deploys capital into a community and wants structure, oversight, and proof behind it.

Build Analog around three sponsor perspectives:

| Sponsor perspective | What they sell | What they need Analog to prove |
|---|---|---|
| eComm Brands | Physical things to many people | IRL participation, product trial, content, community sentiment, category growth, and sales-adjacent proof. |
| Tech Companies | Digital things to fewer, higher-value users or strategic accounts | Credible access to dense professional, creator, builder, executive, or operator communities that can shape adoption and enterprise relationships. |
| Public/Non-profit Funds | Altruistic experiences and outcomes to the public | Access, participation, community formation, civic value, wellness, climate, arts, youth, entrepreneurship, or other measurable public outcomes. |

Everything else should route through these three perspectives. A hospitality group usually behaves like an eComm or local-experience sponsor. A foundation, family office, civic institution, or individual giver usually behaves like a public/non-profit fund. A partner platform may route sponsor demand, but it is not a fourth sponsor perspective unless it is deploying capital itself.

## Universal Sponsor Jobs

Across categories, sponsors hire Analog for five jobs:

| Job | What the sponsor needs |
|---|---|
| Brand safety | Approved opportunities, approved hosts, approved categories, and no surprises in who they partner with. |
| Spend controls and receipts | Trackable line items, spend cards, vendor restrictions, receipts, anomaly detection, and unused-fund visibility. |
| Sentiment and social proof | Evidence that the community welcomed it and the recap circulated. |
| Category growth evidence | Repeat participation, new entrants, category mentions, downstream signups, and habit formation. |
| Operational support | Deployment that is easy to monitor and report from without becoming a new job for the sponsor. |

## eComm Brands

eComm brands sell physical things to many people.

Best fit when they have:

- creator, affiliate, ambassador, or customer-community programs
- products that benefit from trial, ritual, demos, content, or social proof
- existing Shopify, CRM, email, affiliate, or commerce data
- pressure to find growth outside crowded paid social

Primary job:

**Turn trusted digital partners into measurable real-world activations.**

Good Analog plays:

- creator-led classes, runs, dinners, workshops, pickups, or pop-ups
- sponsor-funded attendee benefits
- product sampling tied to attendance or claim flow
- retail, venue, or local experience activations when physical participation drives product behavior
- partner-ad setup state where permissions exist
- post-event proof and renewal recommendation

Special note: eComm Brands that came through CreatorCommerce or a creator/affiliate partner should be evaluated with [CreatorCommerce Demand Bridge](/strategy/creatorcommerce-demand-bridge). Their first Analog motion is usually creator-led IRL, creator attendance, VIP/customer events, or local market-entry activations.

## Tech Companies

Tech companies sell digital things to fewer, higher-value users, buyers, accounts, or ecosystems. The value is often enterprise, strategic, developer, creator, or operator adoption rather than broad consumer reach.

Best fit when they need local or community-shaped adoption rather than broad awareness alone.

Primary job:

**Find the communities that can make a digital product, category, or strategic relationship active in the real world.**

Good Analog plays:

- founder breakfasts
- developer meetups
- creator and operator salons
- city launch events
- campus or professional-community activations
- app install, account creation, or lead proof
- executive dinners, customer councils, partner salons, and strategic account gatherings
- category-building events for AI, fintech, creator tools, commerce infrastructure, dev tools, productivity, or vertical SaaS

## Public/Non-profit Funds

Public and non-profit funds sell altruistic experiences and outcomes to the public. They are not always selling a product; they are selling a reason to gather, participate, improve access, and prove social value.

Best fit when the goal is access, civic participation, wellness, local commerce, youth, climate, arts, entrepreneurship, public health, public space, or community formation.

Primary job:

**Use funding to create or strengthen IRL supply that the market would underfund on its own.**

Good Analog plays:

- microgrants for local organizers
- sponsor-funded free spots or lower ticket prices
- community starter kits
- city lead programs
- venue access funds
- proof reports for civic or philanthropic outcomes
- neighborhood cleanup, conservation, arts, health, youth, climate, entrepreneurship, and local-commerce programs
- public participation reporting for funders, communities, and local partners

## Seed Deck Sponsor Pairings

Use these examples through the three-perspective lens:

| Sponsor | Perspective | Community surface |
|---|---|
| Nike or On-style sponsors | eComm Brands | Local run clubs, walk clubs, recovery rituals |
| Hefty-style sponsors | eComm Brands or Public/Non-profit Funds | Neighborhood trash and cleanup clubs |
| Replit-style sponsors | Tech Companies | Hackathons, builder meetups, technical communities |
| Philanthropists or foundations | Public/Non-profit Funds | Mission-driven programming |
| Environmental non-profits | Public/Non-profit Funds | Hyper-local conservation groups |

## Partner Platforms

Partner platforms are not a fourth sponsor perspective. They can route sponsor-funded demand into qualified supply or deploy one of the three sponsor perspectives through their own surface.

Best fit when they own:

- clubs, communities, venues, ticketing, membership, creator, talent, or operator workflows
- brands or sponsors that want to fund those communities
- enough trust to keep their own GUI and customer relationship

Primary job:

**Embed Analog's sponsorship, brief, proof, and payout rail into an existing partner workflow.**

Good Analog plays:

- partner API sponsorship discovery
- hosted or embedded application forms
- partner-owned sponsorship GUI
- sponsor-safe proof and renewal reporting
- rev share on funded opportunities

## Common Sponsor Questions

| Question | Analog answer |
|---|---|
| Where should we spend? | Use predictive bet logic, host readiness, city supply, sponsor perspective, and category fit. |
| Will the money be used responsibly? | Use event-scoped spend cards, budget limits, transactions, receipts, and spend integrity review. |
| What did we get? | Report proof, market growth, attendance or claim data, content, optional attribution, and renewal logic. |
| Who deserves more money? | Score hosts on market-growth proof, spend stewardship, repeatability, and sponsor trust. |
| Do we need to own the whole UI? | No. Partner APIs can let community, creator, venue, or ticketing platforms keep their own GUI while Analog owns sponsorship intelligence and proof rails. |
