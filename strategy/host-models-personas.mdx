Hosts are the product actors who turn sponsor budget into real-world activity. A host can be a person, organization, venue, community, creator, or local operator.

For Hosts, Analog should feel closer to Airbnb than Shopify. The Host has an under-monetized asset: community trust, a recurring ritual, a room, a venue, a route, a connector network, or local taste. Analog should help that asset earn recurring income while preserving the norms that made it valuable.

Strategically, Analog should model participants by assets and roles, not fixed identity boxes. A creator can also be a host. A venue can also be an organizer. A dot connector can become a city lead. A caterer, photographer, security provider, or rental provider can help originate and complete an activation.

The seed-deck host promise is to move organizers from unpaid or scattered community work into funded, scoped, repeatable work. Analog should remove the worst parts of the current host experience: deals in DMs, scattered cash, reimbursement delays, unclear scale paths, off-brand sponsorship risk, and no compensation for the gathering work itself.

The updated funding promise is cash-based Host enablement. The best Hosts should be able to name what would make the gathering better: coffee, libations, supplies, rentals, affordable space, insurance, print, local design, vendor help, proof capture, or operator time. Analog should fund and prove those needs before pushing product-only support.

## Host Models

| Host model | What they have | What they need | Best first Analog motion |
|---|---|---|---|
| Community organizer | Trust, audience, ritual, local context | Funding, sponsor fit, proof, operational help | Apply through sponsor portal or create Request for Sponsorship |
| Creator or talent manager | Audience, content, product affinity, distribution | IRL concept, space, operator, sponsor package | Run creator-led activation |
| Space owner | Venue, availability, foot traffic, cultural surface | Community, sponsor, programming operator | Location activation or venue-anchored series |
| Dot connector | Relationships across hosts, spaces, vendors, sponsors | Visibility, proof, packaging, and economic upside | City lead, connector profile, or Activation Opportunity workflow |
| Service provider | Execution capacity, food, photo, staffing, rentals, production | Qualified demand, scope, payment confidence | Attach to activation packet or city supply map |
| Local operator | Ability to make events happen repeatedly | Sponsor budget, host inventory, runbooks, proof | City lead, owned activation, or managed pilot |
| Talent manager or creator organization | Roster, audience context, brand relationships | Venue, community, sponsor package, proof | Creator-led activation or partner-originated event pitch |

## Hidden Host Balance Sheet

Organizers often carry material costs before a sponsor ever sees the gathering:

| Cost | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Space and setup | Determines whether the gathering can happen with clear operating constraints and repeatability. |
| Supplies and catering | Makes the event feel generous instead of bare-bones. |
| Vendors and speakers | Raises quality and gives the community more reason to return. |
| Insurance, permits, and waivers | Blocks many otherwise-good activations. |
| Promotion and content | Creates proof, recap momentum, and sponsor visibility. |
| Coordination time | The most common unpaid cost, and the easiest to ignore. |

Analog should make the approved future feel different: make the community asset legible, get matched to aligned Sponsors, use approved spend access without fronting money, build a track record, screen brand fit upfront, and earn operator margin on dependable programs.

## Space Owners

Space owners are important because they can turn physical capacity into sponsor-ready inventory. They can be restaurants, cafes, gyms, galleries, studios, rooftops, coworking spaces, hotels, retailers, entertainment venues, or unused commercial spaces.

They usually need:

- clear use rules
- date and time holds
- capacity and accessibility constraints
- insurance or COI requirements
- sponsor fit guardrails
- a trusted organizer or operator
- proof that programming improves utilization

## Dot Connectors

Dot connectors are high-leverage because they know who should be in the room even when they do not own the room.

They can:

- identify underused spaces
- recruit hosts
- suggest sponsor fits
- find vendors
- route attendees and creators
- spot trust risks
- translate local context into product objects

Treat dot connectors as possible city leads when they can repeatedly unlock host-side options.

Treat the strongest dot connectors and community builders as future talent, not only sourcing labor. See [Media, Connectors, And Funding Engine](/strategy/media-owned-irl-engine) for the status, media, product, and compensation strategy.

## Service Providers

Service providers are marketplace liquidity, not only costs.

Examples:

- caterers and libations providers
- photographers and videographers
- security and extra hands
- production operators
- rentals and equipment providers
- print/signage vendors
- insurance, COI, permit, and waiver navigators
- local designers and print layout partners

They should be captured when they can make an activation feasible, improve sponsor proof, or repeat across city/category playbooks.

## Sponsor-Ready Host Traits

The best early hosts combine:

- audience trust
- permission to monetize
- repeat cadence
- a natural sponsor surface
- data capture readiness
- operator reliability
- operator upside
- willingness to accept proof and spend rules

This is the supply potency formula:

```text
trust x monetization permission x repeat cadence x sponsor surface x data capture x operator reliability x operator upside
```
