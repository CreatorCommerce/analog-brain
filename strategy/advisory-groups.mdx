Analog should build four advisory groups:

- Brand Group
- Software Group (SaaS/AI)
- Community Group
- Non-profit Group

Each group should have 12 to 15 people. The goal is not status. The goal is sharper judgment, warmer access, better fund design, better Host trust, and faster learning in the markets Analog wants to own.

These groups should help Analog see the market from four sides at once:

```text
Brand Group
-> what Sponsors will fund

Software Group
-> what SaaS and AI companies will repeat

Community Group
-> what Hosts actually need and trust

Non-profit Group
-> what public and mission capital can support safely

All four groups
-> better campaigns, better supply, better proof, better product
```

Advisory groups are part of the operating system behind AnalogNYC, AnalogSF, selective brand funds, public grant support, and the Event Prospecting Catalog. They should turn field context into decisions, not create another meeting layer.

## Principle

Advisors should create useful company memory.

That means each advisory input should turn into one of these outputs:

| Advisor input | Analog output |
|---|---|
| Sponsor intro | target account, Sponsor perspective, brief, or fund thesis |
| Host or Venue referral | Event Prospecting Catalog record, Host profile, venue note, or trust signal |
| Category judgment | Sponsor-fit rule, no-go rule, pricing signal, or proof standard |
| Market read | AnalogNYC/AnalogSF dashboard opportunity, media angle, grant shelf update, or city lead note |
| Product feedback | product requirement, workflow change, support template, or launch guardrail |
| Risk concern | compliance note, reputation warning, eligibility check, or escalation path |

If the advice does not become a record, rule, intro, package, or decision, it should not take up operating time.

## Shared Structure

| Rule | Operating model |
|---|---|
| Size | 12 to 15 people per group. Big enough for range, small enough for real contribution. |
| Term | 6 months, renewable when the member is useful. |
| Cadence | 60-minute group call monthly, plus async feedback on specific funds, campaigns, or product questions. |
| Expected contribution | 2 useful intros, leads, or specific market notes per quarter. |
| Primary owner | One Analog operator owns each group and writes the post-call memo. |
| Shared read-ahead | One page: what Analog is testing, what decisions are open, what feedback is needed. |
| Output after each call | 5 to 10 decisions, leads, risks, or product notes entered into the right operating surface. |
| Compensation posture | Start with access, status, early research, event invites, and clear attribution. Add referral, advisory, or commercial economics only when explicit and compliant. |
| Boundary | Advisors inform strategy. They do not control awards, rates, rankings, campaign access, or Host selection. |

Each group needs one owner, one scorecard, one running memo, and one place where leads and notes become product memory.

## Brand Group

The Brand Group helps Analog decide which IRL funds will actually move Sponsor dollars.

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| Who belongs | eComm, CPG, retail, wellness, apparel, beauty, beverage, marketplace, brand partnerships, field marketing, and agency operators. |
| What they know | Which categories need new growth channels, which IRL moments feel brand-safe, what proof a Sponsor needs, and what budgets can move. |
| What they help design | Crocs-style run recovery funds, Midol-style wellness funds, Baden-style rec league funds, local retail funds, community collective co-sponsorships, and proof language for renewals. |
| What they should produce | Sponsor intros, category fund theses, pricing signals, brand-fit warnings, campaign copy, proof standards, and renewal logic. |
| Strong member profile | Someone who has moved partnership, community, field, retail, creator, or experiential budget and knows what makes a brand say yes. |
| Bad fit | A person who likes community abstractly but cannot name budgets, decision-makers, category constraints, or proof requirements. |

The Brand Group should make selective brand funds sharper before the full marketplace is liquid. It should answer: what fund would feel obvious in this market, what would feel fake, and what proof would make the Sponsor fund the next round?

## Software Group (SaaS/AI)

The Software Group helps Analog turn dinners, coffees, salons, meetups, and trusted rooms into repeatable SaaS and AI growth infrastructure.

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| Who belongs | SaaS founders, AI founders, DevRel leaders, ecosystem leads, field marketers, PLG leaders, partner leaders, community leads, and GTM operators. |
| What they know | Why trusted rooms work, how qualified-account density shows up, which communities are valuable, and what makes a room worth sponsoring again. |
| What they help design | Founder dinner funds, developer meetup funds, AI salon programs, partner ecosystem dinners, Cafe Cursor-style room strategy, and account-fit proof. |
| What they should produce | Sponsor leads, ICP benchmarks, qualified-room criteria, SF supply referrals, founder/operator Hosts, content-forward proof ideas, and no-go rules for low-quality rooms. |
| Strong member profile | Someone who knows SaaS acquisition economics and can explain why a room, dinner, or community is worth budget. |
| Bad fit | A generic tech advisor who cannot connect IRL participation to pipeline, partner motion, community trust, or account learning. |

The Software Group should help Analog make SaaS dinners and AI community moments feel like a clean, enjoyable, content-forward CAC channel. The point is not just events. The point is repeatable relationship density.

## Community Group

The Community Group keeps Analog honest on Host trust.

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| Who belongs | Hosts, community builders, run club leaders, dinner club operators, chess club leaders, arts organizers, farmers market operators, venue partners, connectors, and local culture builders. |
| What they know | Real operating costs, hidden balance sheets, growth needs, Sponsor discomfort, local trust, venue dynamics, and what support actually changes behavior. |
| What they help design | Host pitch tools, free-money tools, grant shelves, photo/video perks, QR assets, print inclusion, community collective funds, rates, and Sponsor etiquette. |
| What they should produce | Host referrals, trust reads, supply gaps, useful perk ideas, acceptable Sponsor categories, pricing feedback, venue context, and media opportunities. |
| Strong member profile | Someone who already gathers people, carries real operating costs, and can say plainly what money, proof, media, or services would help. |
| Bad fit | A person who wants platform status but does not operate a real gathering, space, community, or trusted local network. |

The Community Group should make Analog the champion of community. It should pressure-test whether Analog is giving Hosts the tools to earn more, prove more, grow more, and avoid selling out the thing that made the community valuable.

## Non-profit Group

The Non-profit Group helps Analog move public, foundation, and mission capital downstream without creating trust or compliance problems.

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| Who belongs | Nonprofit operators, foundation staff, grant writers, fiscal sponsor operators, BID leaders, civic program leads, cultural institution operators, public-health or youth-program leaders, and impact funders. |
| What they know | Eligibility, regranting, reporting, fraud/waste/abuse controls, board expectations, public trust, mission fit, and local implementation risk. |
| What they help design | Public grant workflows, nonprofit regranting lanes, foundation-backed community funds, civic activation programs, reporting templates, and government workflow pitches. |
| What they should produce | Grant calendars, eligibility rules, agency or foundation intros, reporting standards, fiscal sponsor paths, application packet requirements, and risk boundaries. |
| Strong member profile | Someone who has moved or managed mission capital and knows what makes a funder trust downstream local work. |
| Bad fit | A person who treats grants as generic free money without understanding eligibility, reporting, stewardship, or public accountability. |

The Non-profit Group should make the free-money desk stronger. It should help Analog show Hosts relevant public and private funds, collect the right information once, steward applications with authorization, and eventually sell governments or funders on Analog as workflow infrastructure.

## Composition Target

Each group should be balanced enough to create decisions, not just opinions.

| Seat type | Count per group | Why it matters |
|---|---:|---|
| Budget holders or fund operators | 3 to 4 | They know what money can move and why. |
| Practitioners | 3 to 4 | They know what breaks in the field. |
| Connectors | 2 to 3 | They can create useful introductions into supply, Sponsors, Venues, or institutions. |
| Proof, finance, or operations voices | 1 to 2 | They keep spend, reporting, vendor, and attribution logic real. |
| Taste and culture readers | 2 to 3 | They help Analog avoid bland campaigns and local trust mistakes. |
| Wildcards | 1 to 2 | They create unexpected bridges across categories or cities. |

Prioritize people with real access in NYC and SF first. Add other markets when the operating playbook is ready for expansion.

## How The Groups Feed Analog

| Analog surface | How advisory groups improve it |
|---|---|
| Selective brand funds | Brand and Community advisors pressure-test fund-market fit before Analog publishes the campaign. |
| Sponsor pipeline | Brand and Software advisors identify budget holders, timing, category pain, and proof language. |
| Host supply | Community advisors surface trusted Hosts, Venues, operators, and generated-supply opportunities. |
| Public grant desk | Non-profit advisors identify grant sources, eligibility gaps, reporting needs, and safe application paths. |
| Event Prospecting Catalog | All groups add claimable supply, trust notes, Sponsor fit, and no-go signals. |
| Regional Host dashboard | Advisors help decide which local funds, grants, perks, media asks, and proof prompts should appear. |
| Product primitives | Repeated advisor feedback becomes workflow requirements, not vague market commentary. |
| Media and print | Advisors identify who should be interviewed, featured, listed, invited, or used as a signal of local legitimacy. |
| Proof model | Advisors help define which metrics matter by category: attendance, mentions, qualified accounts, local foot traffic, search lift, impact reporting, or renewal confidence. |

The best sign that the advisory model is working is that the same people help Analog find supply, shape funding, avoid bad fits, and explain proof in language each market already understands.

## 90-Day Rollout

| Time | Action | Output |
|---|---|---|
| Days 1-10 | Write the charter, pick one owner per group, and define the first 3 decisions each group must help with. | group brief, owner, decision list |
| Days 11-20 | Build a 25 to 40 person prospect list per group. | ranked invite list with why-each-person notes |
| Days 21-35 | Recruit 6 to 8 anchor members per group. | first member set and explicit contribution asks |
| Days 36-50 | Run first calls with a one-page read-ahead. | fund ideas, Sponsor leads, Host leads, product notes, risk notes |
| Days 51-70 | Convert outputs into operating surfaces. | catalog records, Sponsor briefs, product tickets, grant notes, no-go rules |
| Days 71-90 | Fill each group toward 12 to 15 members and run second calls. | complete group roster, next-quarter priorities, active intro/fund pipeline |

Start smaller if needed, but do not run a large vague council. Six excellent anchor members are better than 20 passive names.

## Member Scorecard

Use this scorecard before inviting someone.

| Criterion | Good signal |
|---|---|
| Access | They can introduce Analog to real Sponsors, Hosts, Venues, funders, operators, or city leaders. |
| Judgment | They can name what is credible, what is fake, and what would damage trust. |
| Operating evidence | They have actually moved budget, run communities, managed funds, built rooms, or shipped programs. |
| Specificity | They give concrete examples, not broad encouragement. |
| Contribution energy | They are willing to review a brief, make an intro, or share a field read within a quarter. |
| Conflict clarity | Any commercial, investor, agency, Sponsor, or platform conflict is disclosed early. |
| Cross-lane usefulness | They help Analog understand at least one other group better. |

Do not invite people only because the logo looks good. The groups are a market-formation tool, not a social proof wall.

## Governance Rules

Keep the model clean.

| Rule | Why |
|---|---|
| Get permission before using an advisor's name publicly. | Trust matters more than borrowed status. |
| Disclose conflicts when an advisor is tied to a Sponsor, Host, Venue, funder, or vendor. | Analog cannot let advisory access distort marketplace decisions. |
| Do not let advisors choose campaign winners. | Advisors can inform fit; Analog owns allocation rules and fairness. |
| Do not share private Host, Sponsor, or grant information without permission. | Advisory feedback should not leak sensitive operating data. |
| Track advice as inputs, not commands. | Analog needs judgment, not committee governance. |
| Separate referral economics from advisory contribution. | Any compensation tied to deals should be explicit, compliant, and recorded. |

## Success Signals

The advisory system is working when:

- Sponsor briefs become more specific.
- Selective brand funds feel more market-fit before launch.
- Host and Venue referrals get warmer.
- The Event Prospecting Catalog gains trust notes and no-go signals faster.
- The regional Host dashboard shows the right local funds, grants, perks, and media opportunities.
- Public grant workflows get clearer and less bespoke.
- Product decisions get grounded in repeated field reality.
- Analog can explain why a category, market, Host, Venue, or campaign deserves the next dollar.

If the groups produce conversation but not records, leads, rules, or product decisions, shrink the group and raise the contribution bar.
