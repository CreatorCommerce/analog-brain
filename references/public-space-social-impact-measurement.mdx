Use this page when a public, nonprofit, foundation, BID, civic, or public/private Sponsor wants proof that a funded space, event, program, corridor, cleanup, wellness access moment, or community ritual created social impact.

Read this after [Public And Nonprofit Fund Landscape](/references/public-nonprofit-fund-landscape). The funding landscape page explains why public-good capital needs a downstream operating layer. This page explains what public-good proof can measure.

The source pattern is Trust for Public Land and TogetherUp Institute's Community Compass toolkit. The useful idea for Analog:

```text
public space funding
-> inclusive shared space or experience
-> real-time participant feedback
-> belonging, connection, trust, agency, and cross-group contact
-> public-safe proof, program learning, and next-dollar decisions
```

Do not reduce public-space impact to attendance. Attendance says people showed up. It does not say whether the experience helped people feel connected, respected, safe enough to belong, or more willing to participate in community life.

Untaped's public realm evaluation recommendation broadens the measurement frame. Analog should still use Community Compass-style social outcomes for belonging and connection, but public-space funders may also ask for community, placemaking, economic, and fiscal evidence.

Use [Public Space Activation Infrastructure](/references/public-space-activation-infrastructure) when the proof plan depends on permitting, insurance, site plans, weather, vending, concessions, or a public-space stewardship fund.

| Untaped impact category | Analog proof example |
|---|---|
| Community | participation, local access, belonging, trust, agency, cross-group contact, organizer reflections |
| Placemaking | perception of the space, repeat use, safety/comfort perception, cultural visibility, stewardship activity |
| Economic | vendor participation, local business feedback, estimated attendee spend, artist or vendor payments, corridor activity |
| Fiscal | public/private support deployed, permit or fee context, tax or economic modeling only when an evaluation partner supports it |

This does not mean every small Host needs a full evaluation study. The proof design should match the funder's question and the Host's capacity.

## What To Measure

Analog should treat public-space social impact as a mixed proof layer.

| Measure type | What it captures | Analog use |
|---|---|---|
| Participation | who showed up, how many people came, recurrence, claims, check-ins, access created | basic public reporting and funding eligibility |
| New connection | whether participants had meaningful positive interaction with someone they did not already know | proof that the space or event created social contact |
| Cross-difference connection | whether new interactions crossed lines such as race, ethnicity, faith, politics, socioeconomic status, national origin, sexual orientation, or gender identity | proof that the work bridged groups rather than only serving existing circles |
| Belonging | whether people felt they belonged in the space, program, or community | public-good outcome for parks, libraries, third places, youth, arts, wellness, and civic programs |
| Social connection | whether people felt emotionally connected to others present | early indicator for social cohesion and isolation-reduction programs |
| Openness | whether people felt more open to understanding different perspectives | useful for civic, bridge-building, library, culture, and dialogue programs |
| Norms of respect | whether people perceived respectful treatment across differences | public trust, safety, and inclusion signal |
| Agency | whether people felt able to make a positive impact on the people or community around them | civic participation and local stewardship signal |
| Engagement intent | whether people wanted to get more involved in community activities | renewal signal for recurring programs and volunteer pathways |
| Trust | whether people felt more trust toward other attendees, visitors, or community members | social capital signal |
| Desire for cross-group interaction | whether people wanted more contact with people different from them | bridge-building signal |
| Meaningfulness | whether the visit, event, or program felt meaningful | simple quality signal that helps explain why the experience mattered |
| Open-ended reflection | what participants say mattered, what felt missing, and what should improve | qualitative context for funders, Hosts, and operators |

This is not a claim that one survey proves long-term health, economic, safety, or civic outcomes. It is a practical way to capture real-time signals that are closer to those outcomes than a recap deck or attendance count alone.

## Survey Designs

Use the smallest design that can answer the funding question.

| Situation | Survey design | When to collect |
|---|---|---|
| One-time event | post-event survey | immediately after the event, before people leave |
| Single visit to a public space | one-time visit survey | during or right after the visit |
| Multi-session program | pre-survey and post-survey | first session and final session |
| Event where arrival and departure matter | entry survey and exit survey | as people enter and leave |
| Ongoing space or program | repeated one-time surveys plus periodic pre/post cohorts | at consistent moments, with clear sampling notes |

Collect in real time whenever possible. QR surveys are efficient, but paper surveys matter when smartphone access, disability access, language access, or trust make digital forms weaker.

The operating rule:

```text
build feedback into the experience
instead of chasing people after the moment has cooled
```

## Public-Safe Data Rules

Demographic data is useful because it shows who benefits and who may be left out. It is also sensitive.

Use a safety, appropriateness, and comfort test:

| Test | Rule |
|---|---|
| Unsafe | If the question could put someone at risk, do not ask. |
| Inappropriate | If the question does not fit the audience or program, omit it. |
| Uncomfortable but useful | If it helps reveal who benefits and who is left out, ask carefully and explain why. |

Public-space surveys should follow these rules:

- make sensitive questions voluntary
- include a way to skip or prefer not to say
- ask only what will be used
- put demographics at the end
- avoid names, emails, IDs, or unnecessary identifiers in impact surveys
- store and report results in aggregate
- avoid small-cell reporting that could identify people
- pilot wording with community members when the topic or population is sensitive
- keep internal risk notes separate from public summaries

For Analog, raw attendee identity should not become public opportunity data. Use survey results as public-safe summaries, not as a way to expose individuals or sensitive groups.

## Analysis Pattern

A practical public-space report should include:

| Output | What to show |
|---|---|
| Response count | total responses, rough attendance, and collection method |
| Sample notes | whether responses look representative enough to trust, plus known gaps |
| Item averages | average scores for 1-5 agreement items such as belonging, trust, agency, and respect |
| Behavior counts | counts or percentages for new connections and cross-difference connections |
| Demographic splits | only where safe and useful, such as whether belonging differed by age, tenure, race/ethnicity, or resident status |
| Open-ended themes | participant language summarized into practical themes |
| Operator learning | what to keep, change, stop, or fund next |
| Proof boundary | what the data can and cannot claim |

Tie results back to a theory of change or logic model. The question is not only "were scores high?" It is:

```text
Which activity, support, space, or Host behavior appears linked to the outcome,
and what should the Sponsor fund next?
```

## Analog Product Translation

Analog should treat social impact measurement as a proof lane for public-good campaigns, not as a separate analytics product.

| Analog object | Social impact measurement job |
|---|---|
| Campaign | names the public-good thesis, target population, geography, eligible activities, survey design, proof boundary, and renewal rule |
| Event set | groups spaces, public programs, corridors, parks, libraries, cleanups, wellness access, arts, youth, or civic programs by measurement need |
| Application portal | asks Hosts how they will create access, belonging, connection, trust, stewardship, or participation |
| Host event | stores the collection window, QR or paper survey plan, language/access notes, and proof checklist |
| Proof packet | combines spend, receipts, participation, survey summary, open-ended themes, photos where appropriate, and public-safe report language |
| Sponsor data snapshot | shows funders which spaces, programs, Hosts, and formats created the strongest mission-fit evidence |
| Public-safe report | turns the proof packet into a board, donor, agency, public/private coalition, or community update without exposing private data |

Active `cc-analog` can approximate the Host-readiness side with founding grant answers, listing events, reviews, media, organization-owned listing content, and files or operator notes outside the product when needed. The preserved full-platform logic can approximate the funder side with campaigns, event sets, application portals, hosted event pages, QR assets, proof packets, Sponsor data summaries, and operator notes. Future product work can add survey templates, response imports, measure tags, demographic-safety flags, public report builders, and impact dashboards.

## When To Use It

Use this measurement lane for:

- parks, plazas, trails, schoolyards, libraries, recreation centers, and public-space activations
- corridor activation funds and BID programs
- social connection funds
- youth, arts, sports, culture, and recreation programs
- cleanup, stewardship, gardening, and climate action programs
- public health and wellness access funds
- nonprofit downstream funding programs
- community collective funds when contributors want public-good proof

Avoid or add deeper controls when:

- the program touches electioneering, voter contact, advocacy persuasion, or political affiliation data
- the Sponsor wants to claim health, safety, economic, or educational outcomes beyond the data collected
- the sample is too small or biased to report safely
- demographic questions could create risk
- the Host or Sponsor wants raw participant data instead of public-safe summaries

## Sponsor Readout

A good Sponsor readout is cautious and useful:

```text
The funded park series created strong evidence of access and social connection.
Most respondents reported meaningful new interactions, and many reported at least one interaction across difference.
Belonging and trust scores were strongest for repeat attendees.
Teen participation lagged, so the next allocation should add youth programming, earlier event times, and language-access support.
```

A weak readout overclaims:

```text
This event solved loneliness and improved public health.
```

Analog should help Sponsors earn more specific, defensible language.
