Analog's base-case U.S. sponsorship cash-flow TAM is **USD 60.35B per year**.

That number is not the whole event, creator, philanthropy, or public-budget universe. It is the routeable cash-flow layer Analog can credibly attack first:

```text
Sponsor or funder budget
-> trusted Host or community operator
-> governed spend, services, proof, and renewal logic
```

## Model Rules

Use this model carefully.

| Rule | Standard |
|---|---|
| Count cash flow, not software revenue | TAM is the annual Sponsor and funder capital that can be routed to Hosts. Analog ARR depends on pricing and take rate. |
| Use U.S. base case first | The U.S. has enough evidence to answer the venture-scale objection without counting global expansion. |
| Avoid obvious double count | Do not add all creator spend, all experiential spend, all public parks spend, and all philanthropy at full value. Use explicit routeable slices. |
| Keep three lanes separate | Consumer brand, SaaS/B2B, and public/nonprofit dollars have different buyers, proof language, and risk controls. |
| Treat political action as upside | Political action uses similar local supply and proof rails, but it needs separate compliance controls and is not counted here. |
| Treat ticketing and paid event revenue as upside | This model focuses on non-ticket Sponsor and funder capital, which is Analog's sharper wedge. |

Why this lane is bigger than it feels:

- Most people only see sponsorship inside their own niche: a run club, a festival, a podcast, a local event, a creator trip, a retail pop-up, a neighborhood fundraiser, or a conference afterparty.
- The market is not one obvious budget line. It is a budget migration from digital sameness, creator saturation, sampling, retail activation, brand partnerships, and experiential marketing into community moments.
- PQ Media reported that the U.S. experiential market reached USD 64.43B in 2025, with U.S. B2C experiential at USD 47.8B.
- Marketing Brew's Recess story is the live operator signal: Recess quadrupled its community budget, ran or partnered on almost 80 events in May 2026, approached 120 events in June, and used smaller wellness, creativity, and social events to get cans into hands.
- IAB's creator report shows that creator spend is already mainstream, at USD 37B in 2025, but brands still struggle with creator selection, standards, and measurement. Analog can make local Hosts and dot connectors the IRL version of trusted creator supply.

The practical Sponsor range from Analog conversations, USD 10k to USD 3M per year for eComm and consumer brands, fits this market. The important part is not the exact first check. It is that the repeatable budget pool exists and is moving toward smaller, higher-touch IRL.

| Component | Hard-data anchor | Routeable assumption | Modeled cash flow |
|---|---:|---:|---:|

| Conferences, seminars, promotions, attendance | U.S. B2B experiential was USD 16.63B in 2025; after exhibit space rentals, the remaining B2B experiential pool is USD 6.52B | Count as B2B event budget already intended for relationship, education, partnership, and pipeline creation | USD 6.52B |
| **Total** |  |  | **USD 16.63B** |

This lane intentionally absorbs event marketing.

The Analog point of view is that SaaS event spend will not disappear. It will fragment and professionalize. Large events, trade shows, and conferences are expensive. SaaS teams still need trust, pipeline, partner learning, developer adoption, customer councils, executive access, and ecosystem gravity. The budget shifts toward smaller repeatable rooms when the room has proof.

The hard signals:

- PQ Media put U.S. B2B experiential at USD 16.63B in 2025, with exhibit space rental alone at USD 10.11B.
- Cvent's 2026 Event-Led Growth research says 68% of marketers call events their most effective channel, 83% say events generate steady revenue, 72% say deals close faster when prospects attend events, and 74% can trace pipeline directly to event touchpoints.
- Cvent also reports the average number of events hosted per year dropped from 29 in 2024 to 16 in 2025. The read is not "events are dead." It is "volume is being replaced by significance."
- a16z's Tech Week motion shows the same shift at city scale. In 2025, New York Tech Week drew 40,000 attendees across 1,020 events. In 2026, the program was described as roughly 40,000 attendees across 1,500 events.
- SaaS Capital's 2026 private B2B SaaS benchmark says the median private B2B SaaS company spends 8% of ARR on marketing and 15% on selling costs. That validates the existence of serious GTM budgets even before customer success, community, and partner budgets are considered.
- Gartner forecast global cloud application services, SaaS, end-user spending of USD 299.071B in 2025, up 19.2%. That is not used directly in the TAM total, but it shows the underlying SaaS market has enough economic mass to support a durable event/community budget layer.

The Smile.io pattern, pulling roughly USD 80k out of big events to run 80 smaller USD 1k events, is exactly the motion this lane models. Analog should not need SaaS companies to invent a new behavior. It should help them re-route existing field, event, community, and ecosystem budget into trusted Hosts with proof.

This lane is easy to undercount because the money does not always look like "sponsorship."

It may be a city agency, BID, parks department, foundation, community foundation, corporate social impact program, hospital, university, public/private coalition, or nonprofit regranting program. But the job is similar: fund trusted people close to participation, then prove that money created access, connection, play, stewardship, culture, local commerce, or civic value.

Live examples make the lane concrete:

- NYC Open Play provides free, staffed overnight pickup soccer at one field in every borough during the World Cup window, with New York City FC, NYC Footy, NYC Parks, Hudson River Park Trust, and StubHub involved.
- NYC Soccer Streets uses a public/private/nonprofit stack: the Mayor's Office, NYC DOT, NYC Public Schools, Street Lab, and Chobani enabling 50 school-block soccer celebrations.
- Chobani's Feed the Dream program makes the national brand-side version explicit: 500 youth soccer clubs can receive USD 10k sponsorships, a USD 5M equivalent sponsorship pool.
- The NYC Green Fund Grassroots program, managed by City Parks Foundation, provides USD 1k to USD 40k per year for up to two years to community groups and organizations activating public parks and open spaces.
- Revson Foundation describes the NYC Green Fund as a pooled fund that redistributes roughly USD 2.5M per year and raised/distributed more than USD 8.6M from 2020 to 2023.
- New York State's NY KICKS program offers up to USD 5M in grants for community soccer fields.
- Portland's Vibrant Spaces Community Events Activation Funds committed USD 2.3M for community event activation from ARPA local recovery funds.

Analog should treat this as a supply-capture and public-trust lane. Public and nonprofit funders already have mission capital. The gap is downstream: finding trusted Hosts, keeping spend allowable, reducing reporting burden, and proving local outcomes.

## Sensitivity

The base case is intentionally narrower than the gross budget universe.

| Case | Consumer brand | SaaS/B2B | Gov/nonprofit | Aggregate |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Conservative floor | USD 23.92B | USD 4.16B | USD 8.43B | USD 36.51B |
| Base case | USD 28.16B | USD 16.63B | USD 15.56B | USD 60.35B |
| Expansion case | USD 47.80B | USD 23.90B | USD 45.00B | USD 116.70B |

How to read the cases:

| Case | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Conservative floor | Count only B2C event sponsorships, 25% of B2B experiential, and a tighter public/nonprofit slice. |
| Base case | Count B2C event sponsorship plus small creator/live-event shifts, full U.S. B2B experiential as migration-eligible, and modest fractions of public/nonprofit gathering-adjacent pools. |
| Expansion case | Count the full U.S. B2C experiential pool, broader SaaS/tech marketing and customer/community budget migration, and deeper public/nonprofit grant program routing. This still excludes political action and global expansion. |

## Strategic Read

The TAM answer is not "there is one giant sponsorship budget sitting in a known category."

The TAM answer is:

```text
IRL community funding is currently fragmented across experiential,
creator, event, SaaS field, public, philanthropic, and civic budgets.
Analog's company opportunity is to make that fragmented cash flow
findable, governable, attributable, and repeatable.
```

This directly answers the two investor objections:

| Concern | Better answer |
|---|---|
| Sponsorship dollars feel small | They feel small because people only experience the local slice of the market. The hard-data pools behind the behavior are already tens of billions in the U.S. alone. |
| GTM and community acquisition are fragmented | The fragmentation is the wedge. Communities need to be found like businesses, funded like operators, governed like partners, and proven like a repeatable channel. |

The company bet is that discovery comes before funding.

Hosts, communities, local rituals, venues, and operators need public-safe profiles, proof records, city context, and funding readiness. Once they can be found, Sponsors and funders can allocate better. Once money flows, proof and renewal make the market safer. That is how the directory, discovery layer, sponsorship rail, attribution rail, and event-platform integrations become one company instead of a services marketplace.
