The current IRL software market is making it easier to create, share, ticket, and discover events. That matters, but it is not the hardest problem.

Read this after [Macro Trends Behind The IRL Economy](/references/macro-trends-irl-economy). The macro page explains why IRL matters now. This page explains why the existing event software stack still leaves room for Analog. Then read [eComm Brand Landscape](/references/ecomm-brand-landscape) for the Sponsor-side commerce stack.

The harder market is the transition from hobby to funded operator.

```text
easy invite tools
-> more gatherings get created
-> more Hosts carry real cost and operational risk
-> vertical marketplaces try to monetize slices of the market
-> the core gap remains: who funds the Host, lowers friction, proves value, and renews the next dollar?
```

For Hosts and organizers, the sharper analogy is Airbnb: a person has an under-monetized asset that was not originally built as inventory. Analog should help them monetize that asset, their trusted community, in a recurring way that feels safe, respected, and additive.

For Sponsors, the experience should feel closer to calling a ride or booking a stay: clear request, trusted supply, known rules, easy approval, and confidence that someone qualified will deliver. Behind that simplicity, Analog uses a hard-earned network, spend rails, proof workflows, and human judgment to route funds to trusted Hosts and connectors.

Shopify remains useful as the broader operating-layer analogy. Shopify did not teach every brand how to make a product; it became the layer brands use when they are ready to sell, manage, finance, and grow. Analog should become that operating layer once a Host's community is real enough to fund, professionalize, and repeat.

## Landscape Map

| Layer | Examples | What they commoditize | What remains unsolved |
|---|---|---|---|
| Invite and RSVP | Apple Invites, Partiful | Attractive invitations, RSVP flow, guest updates, photos, memories, casual event coordination | Host funding, sponsor fit, spend governance, proof, repeat economics |
| Event page and community calendar | Luma | Event pages, invites, calendars, ticketing, blasts, guest management, check-in, discovery | Who pays for the gathering, why the Host should keep doing it, and what earns renewal |
| Ticketing and event marketing | Posh | Ticket types, event pages, attendee data, payouts, marketing pixels, SMS/email, marketplace discovery | Non-ticket revenue, community trust, sponsor capital, public/nonprofit capital, Host working capital |
| Vertical IRL monetization | DNNR | Branded dinner clubs, matching, restaurant logistics, recurring paid dinners | The model is powerful but narrow; not every community is a dinner club |
| Brand/host product marketplace | onbrand | Host applications to brands, product shipments, brand-safe review, product trial, recaps | Product support is useful, but cash, services, tools, and renewal economics are the deeper Host need |

These companies make the behavior normal: ordinary people, creators, communities, and brands are moving more activity into software-mediated IRL. They also make the invite page less defensible as the center of the market.

## The Invite Is Becoming Commodity

Apple entering invitations is a market signal. When the operating system can make a good-looking invite, the invite itself cannot be the whole company thesis.

Partiful, Luma, Apple Invites, and Posh each make some version of "create the event and get people there" easier. The category will keep improving: prettier pages, faster RSVP, better guest lists, better reminders, better discovery, more AI-assisted setup.

Analog should assume that basic event creation gets cheaper and easier every year. That is good for Analog. More invites mean more Hosts discovering the same next question:

```text
How do I pay for this, make it better, prove it worked, and keep doing it?
```

## Strategic Pressure Test

The threat is not that invite tools replace Analog. The threat is that the market learns to expect the visible event layer to be cheap, beautiful, and fast.

| Pressure | Analog response |
|---|---|
| Invite tools add more ticketing, payments, photos, AI, and guest features. | Do not fight the front-door war. Ingest attendance, RSVPs, links, claims, and proof from wherever the Host already gathers people. |
| Ticketing tools own paid event revenue. | Focus on non-ticket capital: Sponsor funds, public/nonprofit funds, macro grants, services/tools, print, and renewal underwriting. |
| Vertical marketplaces win specific lanes such as dinners or product gifting. | Partner, route demand, or use them as supply sources when useful; avoid cloning narrow lanes that do not solve cash-based Host economics. |
| A larger platform tries to become "Shopify for IRL." | Analog needs the hard parts first: trusted supply, funding rails, spend/proof history, city media distribution, and underwriting memory. |

## The Airbnb And Shopify Frame

Use both analogies carefully.

| Analogy | What it clarifies | Where it breaks |
|---|---|---|
| Airbnb for Hosts | A trusted community, venue, ritual, or connector network is a latent asset that can become recurring income. | Communities are not empty rooms. The Host must control fit, consent, brand presence, and trust. |
| Ride or stay ease for Sponsors | A Sponsor should be able to request trusted IRL access with clear rules, proof, and delivery confidence. | The backend is not simple: supply trust, spend governance, local context, and proof require Analog's network and rails. |
| Shopify for the market | Once a Host has something real, Analog can provide the operating layer: capital, tools, proof, workflow, partners, and scale. | Analog should not imply Hosts are simply storefronts or that community trust is generic inventory. |

The Shopify analogy is about the transition point, not the Host identity.

| Shopify world | Analog world |
|---|---|
| A brand has a product and wants to sell, manage, finance, and scale. | A Host has a recurring ritual, room, audience, venue, or local trust surface and wants to fund, professionalize, and repeat it. |
| Shopify provides the commerce operating layer and partner ecosystem. | Analog provides the funding, spend, proof, sponsor/public capital, and services/tools operating layer. |
| Apps, payments, capital, logistics, and storefronts help brands become businesses. | Micro grants, macro grants, spend cards, proof packets, files/docs, insurance, print, vendors, media, and fund pages help Hosts become funded operators. |
| Brands bring products; Shopify helps them monetize and scale. | Hosts bring trust; Analog helps them monetize without flattening the community into inventory. |

Analog does not need to own every invite, ticket, calendar, POS, venue, insurance, publication, or community tool. It needs to own the funding convention, trusted-supply network, and product memory around the Host's transition into durable work.

## Why Vertical Marketplaces Are Not Enough

DNNR and onbrand are useful signals because they are closer to monetization than pure invite tools.

DNNR shows that recurring dinners can become a branded, paid, operationally supported product. onbrand shows that brands want to enter real moments and that Hosts want support from brands they like.

The limitation is breadth. A dinner club platform is not the whole IRL economy. A product-shipment marketplace is not the whole sponsorship economy. Both can work inside their lanes while skipping the market-wide problem:

```text
How do many different Hosts make enough money,
get enough operational support,
prove enough value,
and keep participating in IRL without turning it into a loss-leading fad?
```

Analog should learn from these tools, partner where useful, and avoid becoming a vertical feature clone.

## Analog's Wedge

Analog's wedge is not "better invitations." It is the funding layer after a Host has proven that people care.

The product should help answer:

| Question | Analog answer |
|---|---|
| Who should fund this? | eComm Brand, SaaS/Tech Company, civic/public/nonprofit funder, political action Sponsor, foundation, AI-UBI-like fund, or mixed capital stack. |
| What should money pay for? | Coffee, libations, space, rentals, supplies, print, local designers, insurance, vendor support, operator time, or access. |
| How does the Host stay safe? | Sponsor fit, consent, controlled spend, contracts/docs, approved categories, privacy rules, and clear scope. |
| How does the Sponsor stay safe? | Trusted supply, spend governance, proof packets, sentiment, participation data, anomaly review, and renewal logic. |
| What compounds? | Host history, catalog records, proof quality, city knowledge, sponsor trust, media assets, and repeatable funding playbooks. |

## What This Means For Strategy

Do not compete where the market is already racing toward commodity. Let invite, ticketing, and vertical tools keep expanding the number of people doing IRL.

Analog should focus on the next layer:

- fund Hosts with cash, services, tools, and proof support
- make Sponsor demand feel easy to request while Analog handles trusted supply, spend rails, and delivery proof behind the scenes
- help Sponsors and public/nonprofit funders deploy capital safely
- capture enough spend, proof, sentiment, and field context to underwrite renewal
- build an Event Prospecting Catalog that remembers which Hosts, rituals, and cities are becoming fundable
- let future capital sources evolve: brands, tech companies, municipalities, foundations, nonprofits, public/private coalitions, or AI-UBI-like funds

The market education takeaway:

```text
The invite layer helps IRL happen.
The ticketing layer helps IRL transact.
The vertical marketplace helps one lane monetize.
Analog should help the whole Host economy become funded, trusted, and repeatable.
```
